Aegean Coast Escape

Duration8 days Best SeasonMay to June, September to October BudgetMid range PaceSlow

An eight day coastal trip designed for slowing down. The Aegean does not reward rushing. You start in Izmir for the city food and the ancient sites, then drift south to the Muğla coast where the small towns each offer a different rhythm. Bodrum has the nightlife and the gulet harbour, Fethiye has the nature and the Lycian trail starts twenty minutes away, Datça is the quiet one, and Marmaris is where you go if you want a busier seaside town. The summer light here is unlike anywhere else in the country, and a private gulet for two days is the kind of expense that earns its cost in memory.

Day 1

Izmir, the Aegean Capital

Land in Izmir and base on the Kordon, the seaside promenade. The hotels along the front face west, the sun sets over the bay, the air cools by seven and the city comes alive.

Late afternoon, walk the Kemeraltı bazaar. The market in central Izmir runs into itself like a working Levantine souk, less polished than the Grand Bazaar in Istanbul but more lived in. Eat boyoz, the Sephardic pastry that came with the Jews who fled Spain in 1492 and is still made the same way.

Dinner along the Kordon, slow start, the trip is paced for it. Order a meze spread, a fish, drink rakı with water on the side.

Day 2

Izmir, Ephesus and Şirince

Day trip south to Ephesus, the largest Roman city ruin in the eastern Mediterranean. An hour and a half from Izmir. Get there early before the cruise ship buses, which start arriving around ten. Three hours minimum inside.

The Library of Celsus is the famous photograph but the residential Terrace Houses are where the real value is, ticketed separately, with intact mosaics from the Roman elite homes built into the hillside.

Lunch in Şirince, the wine village in the hills above Ephesus. A Greek village abandoned in 1923 and partially repopulated, now full of family run wineries and restaurants. The fruit wines here range from genuinely good to very strange, taste before you buy.

Afternoon, drive back to Izmir along the coast, evening on the Kordon.

Day 3

Izmir to Bodrum

Drive south to Bodrum, around three and a half hours via the coast. Stay in Yalıkavak if you want the high end marina mood, or in Türkbükü for boutique hotels in a quieter cove. The town centre of Bodrum itself is busy but worth one walk for the castle.

Settle in, swim before sunset. The water in this part of the Aegean is the clearest of the Turkish coast, mineral blue rather than turquoise.

Dinner in a small cove restaurant. Try levrek the Aegean sea bass, or çipura sea bream, both are line caught locally and the best way to eat them is grilled simple, with olive oil and lemon.

Day 4

Bodrum, Day on Water

Take a boat. Bodrum's gulets, the wooden Turkish sailing boats, are the way to see the peninsula's coves. Karaada, Akvaryum Bay, and the Black Island are the standard stops, with mud baths in a thermal cave on Karaada that are actually fun.

Six islands shared boat is the cheap option, a private gulet for four to six guests starts around 400 EUR for the day. Worth it if you can split.

Lunch is on board, usually grilled fish caught at one of the swim stops. Back to the marina by late afternoon.

Evening, walk the marina. Bodrum is at its best after dark, when the cafes along the waterfront fill up and the white houses on the hill catch the moonlight.

Day 5

Bodrum to Datça

Drive south to Datça, around two hours. Or take the seasonal car ferry from Bodrum to Datça in summer, which is a third of the time and more enjoyable.

Datça peninsula is the quiet one. Less developed, fewer hotels, fewer restaurants, more genuine. The town itself is a sleepy harbour, a few good fish places, a lazy waterfront.

Settle in, walk the harbour, swim. Tomorrow is for ruins.

Day 6

Datça, Knidos and Coves

Drive to Knidos at the western tip of the Datça peninsula, around an hour through pine forest. This was a Greek port city in antiquity, famous in its time for the marble Aphrodite statue by Praxiteles, the first life sized female nude in Greek art. The statue is gone but the city's foundations remain, two harbours flanking a peninsula, with steep stone steps connecting them. Allow two hours and good shoes.

Lunch at one of the small fish restaurants near the Knidos harbour, then back to Datça by sunset.

Quiet dinner in town. The peninsula is for slowing down, lean into it.

Day 7

Datça to Fethiye

Drive east to Fethiye, around three hours. The road climbs through pine and overlooks the sea on the way. Fethiye is the largest of the southern Aegean towns, with a working harbour and a real local population that does not entirely rely on tourism.

Ölüdeniz blue lagoon is twenty minutes south of town and the famous photograph. The lagoon itself is a national park, ticketed, with calm shallow water and the contrast of pine forest behind. The strip of beach outside the lagoon is free and just as good for swimming.

Late afternoon, drive up to Babadağ, the mountain above Ölüdeniz where paragliders take off. Even if you do not jump, the cable car to the top has the longest views in the region. The 1700 metre summit gives you the entire Lycian coast on one side, the inland valleys on the other.

Dinner in Fethiye, walk the marina.

Day 8

Fethiye, Departure

If your flight is late, drive to Kayaköy, the abandoned Greek village above Fethiye. The population was exchanged in 1923 and the houses have stood empty since, slowly losing roofs to weather. Walk through it as the morning light hits the stone.

Or if you want to swim once more, Saklıkent Gorge is forty minutes inland, an eighteen kilometre canyon that is walkable in summer when the water is low. Lunch at one of the riverside places suspended over the stream.

Dalaman airport is forty minutes from Fethiye centre, with direct flights to most of Europe.

General Notes

Driving and ferries. A rental car is essential for this route, distances on the map look short but mountain roads add time. The Bodrum to Datça car ferry runs in summer only, mid May to mid October, and saves three hours of driving. Book in advance during peak weeks. Drop the car in Dalaman or Antalya at the end.

Where to stay. Kordon waterfront in Izmir. Türkbükü or Yalıkavak in Bodrum for boutique mood, Bodrum centre for nightlife. Datça village or one of the cove hotels nearby. Fethiye centre or Ölüdeniz strip for proximity to the lagoon.

Season. Late May to early July, then mid September to mid October. The shoulders. July and August add heat and crowds, but the water is at its warmest. Outside this window the boats stop running and several hotels close.

Boats. If you can afford it, charter a private gulet for two or three days. The cost split four ways is roughly the same as a four star hotel night each, and the experience of moving cove to cove with your own boat is the version of the trip people remember.

Pace. Eight days at this slow pace is the right shape. Cutting to six means dropping Datça, which is fine if quiet is not the goal. Adding more time, the natural extension is east along the Lycian coast toward Kaş and Kalkan.
