Curated Route · 05

Eastern Heritage

Duration9 days Best SeasonMarch to May, October to November BudgetMid range PaceBalanced

The southeast is where most foreign visitors stop, and where Türkiye's history goes the deepest. This is the part of the country that feels closer to Mesopotamia than to Europe. Şanlıurfa claims to be Abraham's birthplace and has the cave to prove it, plus Göbekli Tepe nearby, the world's oldest known temple complex at roughly 11,500 years old. Mardin sits on a cliff overlooking the Mesopotamian plain, an ochre stone city of Syriac, Arab, Kurdish and Turkish layers, where churches and mosques are still active down the same alley. Gaziantep is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on earth and arguably the country's strongest food. Nine days is enough to slow down and let each place register. The pace is balanced, the comfort is good, the photographs are unlike anywhere else in Türkiye.

Day 1

Arrival in Gaziantep

Fly into Gaziantep, the practical entry point for the southeast. Most international travellers connect through Istanbul. The drive from the airport into town is twenty minutes.

Base in Bey Mahallesi, the old quarter with stone houses restored as boutique hotels. The streets here are best on foot, narrow and cool in summer because the houses are built tightly together for shade.

Settle in, walk a little, get oriented. First dinner at Imam Çağdaş, the kebab and lahmacun house that has been running for over a century. Order shareable, finish with katmer from Orkide Pastanesi if you have room.

Day 2

Gaziantep, Mosaics and Markets

Morning at the Zeugma Mosaic Museum. The largest mosaic museum in the world, holding floor mosaics from a Roman city now under a dam reservoir. Allow two hours minimum, the Gypsy Girl alone deserves the time.

Lunch at a kebapçı, try ali nazik, the smoked aubergine puree topped with diced lamb that is canonically from Antep. Drink ayran with it.

Afternoon at the Bakırcılar Çarşısı, the copper market where workshops still hammer pots by hand. Walk through to the spice market and the Gümrük Hanı, the old caravanserai that now houses small craftsmen.

Evening, an evening walk up to the Tarihi Gümrük Hanı, the old customs caravanserai in the market district. Order a menengiç coffee in one of the courtyard cafes as the light drops. The atmosphere here at dusk is part of why people fall in love with Antep.

Day 3

Gaziantep to Şanlıurfa

Drive east to Şanlıurfa, around two hours. The city sits on the edge of the Mesopotamian plain, the air gets drier as you go.

Lunch in Urfa. Cevahir Han in the old town, set in a restored caravanserai courtyard, is a comfortable place to start. Try çiğ köfte, the raw bulgur and pepper paste version that is canonically from this region, traditionally served wrapped in lettuce leaves with lemon.

Afternoon at the Balıklıgöl, the sacred carp pool in the old town. Local tradition holds this is where Abraham was thrown into a fire by Nimrod, and the fire turned to water and the firewood to fish. The pool is full of fat carp considered sacred, fed by visitors. The complex around it includes the cave of Abraham and several Ottoman mosques.

Stay in the old town, in a restored Urfa house if possible. Dinner with traditional music at a sıra gecesi place if you can find one happening tonight. These are local musical evenings with food and rakı, originally a tradesmen's gathering, now opened to outsiders at certain venues.

Day 4

Şanlıurfa, Göbekli Tepe

Half day at Göbekli Tepe, twenty minutes by taxi from the city. This is the oldest known temple complex in the world, dated to roughly 9500 BCE, predating Stonehenge by seven thousand years and the invention of agriculture by a thousand. The site changed how archaeologists think about the origins of civilisation. The standing T shaped pillars carved with animals are the famous photographs, but seeing the scale in person is what registers.

Plan two hours on site, longer if you want to walk between the excavated enclosures. There is a small visitor centre with context boards, take time with these before walking the site, the why of the place is more interesting than the what.

Back to Urfa for late lunch. Try a local Urfa kebapçı for the local Urfa kebap, more peppery than Antep, drier in texture.

Afternoon, the Şanlıurfa Archaeology Museum, which holds many of the original Göbekli Tepe artefacts including the Urfa Man, the oldest known life sized human statue at around 11,500 years. Two hours.

Evening, walk the Mevlid i Halil mosque area as the sun sets over the old town. The light on the limestone walls is the photograph.

Day 5

Şanlıurfa to Mardin via Harran

Morning detour south to Harran, an hour from Urfa. This is one of the oldest continuously inhabited places on earth, mentioned in Genesis. The famous beehive houses, conical mud brick domes that are essentially passive cooling for the desert climate, are still occupied in parts of the village. Walk through, talk to a family if invited, this is genuinely lived in landscape rather than a museum.

Lunch back in Urfa or on the road, then drive to Mardin, around three hours northeast. The road climbs out of the plain and the geography changes, you start to see the hills that mark the southeastern edge of Anatolia.

Arrive Mardin late afternoon. The old town is built into a south facing cliff, looking out over the Mesopotamian plain. Honey coloured limestone houses stack down the slope, with terraces that catch the evening light. Stay in the old town, in a stone house hotel.

Walk the main street at sunset, the views southward are as good as travel views get. Dinner at one of the rooftop terraces.

Day 6

Mardin, Stone City

Morning walk through the Mardin old town from west to east. Side alleys here are nearly perpendicular to the main street and worth following. The architecture mixes Syriac, Armenian, Arab and Ottoman elements layered over each other, often within a single building. The Sultan Isa Medresesi from the fourteenth century has the most photographed doorway in the city.

Lunch at a Süryani, the Syriac Christian community here is small but still active. Try kibe, similar to içli köfte but specifically Syriac, and harire the bulgur soup. Most restaurants serve some local wine, the southeast has been making wine for thousands of years.

Afternoon, drive to Deyrulzafaran Monastery, fifteen minutes outside Mardin. A Syriac Orthodox monastery founded in the fifth century, still active, the home base of the Syriac Patriarch until the early twentieth century. The yellow stone gives the place its name, deyr zaferan, the saffron monastery.

Back to Mardin for dinner. The terraces along the cliff side are at their best after sunset, with the lights of villages stretching into the plain below.

Day 7

Mardin, Midyat and Tur Abdin

Day trip east to Midyat, around an hour. Smaller, quieter than Mardin, also stone built, with several active Syriac neighbourhoods and silver workshops where filigree silver is still made by hand.

Continue to Mor Gabriel Monastery, the oldest active Syriac Orthodox monastery in the world, founded in 397. Still functioning, with monks living and praying there. The afternoon visit is more relaxed than the morning one. Modest dress required.

If you have energy, Note: Hasankeyf is now largely submerged under the Ilısu Dam reservoir. The old town and most historic structures are underwater. It is no longer a practical destination for this route. This adds a long day, decide based on appetite.

Back to Mardin for dinner. Tonight is your last in stone.

Day 8

Mardin to Gaziantep, Long Drive Day

Drive back to Gaziantep, around four hours, longer if you stop. This is a transit day but the road through southeastern Anatolia is part of the experience. Watch for the geography flattening into agricultural plain as you go west.

Optional stop at Halfeti, the half submerged village along the Euphrates dam reservoir, an hour off the main road. Take a small boat onto the water, drink tea at a riverside place. The minaret of the submerged village still rises above the waterline, which is the photograph.

Arrive Gaziantep late afternoon. Last evening in Antep, a long meyhane dinner, more rakı than you planned to drink, the way good last nights tend to go.

Day 9

Gaziantep, Departure

Slow morning. If your flight is late, breakfast at Tahmis, the historic coffeehouse, with a final menengiç kahvesi, the wild pistachio coffee.

Last walk through the copper market. Buy something heavy you will use, a copper pan or coffee pot, this is one of the few places in Türkiye where the artisanal claim still holds.

Antep airport is twenty minutes from the centre. Direct flights to Istanbul, several to European capitals.

General Notes

Flights. Fly into and out of Gaziantep is the simplest, the route loops back to where you started. Antep airport flies direct to Istanbul roughly hourly, plus a few European destinations. If your home airport routes you through Istanbul anyway, this saves a day.

Where to stay. Bey Mahallesi in Gaziantep, the restored stone house hotels. Old town in Şanlıurfa, ideally a restored Urfa house. Old town in Mardin, on the cliff side facing south for the views.

Driving distances. All driving is on good roads. Antep to Urfa is two hours, Urfa to Mardin three. The drive back from Mardin to Antep is four to five depending on stops. A rental car is the right choice for this route, public transport works but adds half days.

Season. Mid March to early June, mid September to early November. Summers are hot, often 40 plus, and the stone cities radiate heat into the night. Winters are surprisingly cold with some snow in Mardin. The shoulder seasons are perfect.

Cultural notes. Modesty matters more in the southeast than on the Aegean. Cover shoulders and knees in the religious sites. Alcohol is available but less prominent than in Istanbul. The food is heavier on lamb and chickpeas, lighter on seafood. Most people speak Turkish, but Arabic, Kurdish and Syriac are also active in different neighbourhoods.

Regional note. Parts of southeastern Türkiye were affected by the February 2023 earthquakes. Gaziantep and Şanlıurfa have largely recovered, but infrastructure outside city centres may still show signs of reconstruction. Border provinces (Hatay, Kilis) should be checked for current travel advisories.
