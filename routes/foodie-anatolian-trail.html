Curated Route · 03

Foodie Anatolian Trail

Duration10 days Best SeasonYear round, best September to November BudgetMid range PaceBalanced

Three cities, three completely different food traditions, ten days to eat through them properly. Istanbul brings the imperial kitchen, the seafood meyhanes, and the immigrant communities that built modern Turkish street food. Gaziantep is one of the deepest food cities on earth, the home of pistachio baklava, the only city outside France in UNESCO's gastronomy network for good reason. Izmir's Aegean kitchen runs on olive oil, herbs, and what comes out of the sea, lighter and more vegetal than anywhere else in the country. You will eat three meals a day for ten days, take cooking classes, walk markets at dawn, and leave with a sense of why food in Türkiye is regional in a way most cuisines are not.

Day 1

Istanbul, the Bosphorus Kitchen

Land in Istanbul, base in Karaköy or Galata. The food scene here is denser than Sultanahmet, less touristy, and within walking distance of the markets and the Bosphorus.

Late afternoon, walk the Karaköy fish market, then a coffee at Karabatak, the third wave coffee shop that more or less started Istanbul's specialty coffee scene.

Dinner at a meyhane in Beyoğlu. The full meyhane experience runs three hours minimum. Order meze, then grilled fish, then more meze. Drink rakı with water on the side. The waiters will tell you to slow down and they are right.

Day 2

Istanbul, Markets and Manti

Morning at the Spice Bazaar, then the streets behind it where the actual food vendors are. Buy pul biber the Aleppo style red pepper flakes, sumac for the trip home, a small bag of pistachios from Antep. Vendors will let you taste before buying.

Lunch is manti, the small handmade Turkish dumplings, typically served with garlic yoghurt and brown butter. The version in Istanbul comes from Kayseri, which you would not eat in Antep or Izmir, regional disambiguation matters here.

Afternoon, take the ferry to Kadıköy on the Asian side. The market street here is a working market, no tourists. Eat ciğer grilled lamb liver from a street stand, then kumpir stuffed potato from one of the standing kiosks at the waterfront. Walk back to the ferry pier as the sun drops over the European skyline.

Dinner light, you have an early flight.

Recommended experiences
Kadıköy market food tour Spice Bazaar guided tasting
Day 3

Istanbul to Gaziantep

Morning flight to Gaziantep, around an hour and a half. Settle in, the city centre has several boutique hotels in restored stone houses around Bey Mahallesi.

Late afternoon, walk to the Zeugma Mosaic Museum. The largest mosaic museum in the world, with second and third century AD floor mosaics rescued from a Roman city now under a dam reservoir. The Gypsy Girl mosaic is the famous one. Two hours.

First Antep dinner. Çulcuoğlu for kebabs, or Imam Çağdaş which is probably the most famous lahmacun and kebab house in the country. Order a few of everything, share, the portions are smaller than Istanbul. Finish with katmer, the buttery pistachio pastry that is unique to Antep, only at Orkide Pastanesi if you want the canonical version.

Day 4

Gaziantep, Baklava Day

Breakfast is a serious meal in Antep. Find a katıklı ekmek place, the local breakfast bread, eaten with cheese and olives. Or go to Tahmis, the historic coffeehouse for menengiç kahvesi, the wild pistachio coffee that is local to the region.

Mid morning, a baklava workshop. The dough is rolled to translucence, the pistachios are local Antep, the butter is unsalted, the syrup uses minimal sugar. Most workshops will let you watch and try, some will let you roll dough yourself.

Lunch at a kebapçı for ali nazik, smoked aubergine puree topped with diced lamb, and simit kebabı, the Antep style with bulgur. Compare with the kebab you ate yesterday at the next place over.

Afternoon at the Bakırcılar Çarşısı, the copper market. Workshops still hammering pots and pans by hand, the sound carries down the lane. Pick up a small cezve, the long handled coffee pot, as a thing you will use.

Dinner with beyran, the breakfast soup of Antep made with rice, lamb, and a dark broth. Most places stop serving by noon. Metanet Lokantası opens early, three thirty in the morning officially, finishes by lunch. If you want it for dinner you go to a different style of restaurant that does evening service. Ask your hotel.

Recommended experiences
Baklava making workshop in Gaziantep Antep food and coffee walking tour
Day 5

Gaziantep, Cooking Class and Halfeti

Morning cooking class. Most schools in town will run a half day around three or four dishes, often içli köfte the stuffed bulgur shells, aşure the multi grain dessert, and a kebab variant. The hands on time matters more than the recipe sheet.

Afternoon, drive to Halfeti, around two hours east. The old town is partially submerged under a dam reservoir, the rest of the village sits along the new lakeshore. Take a small boat out on the water, drink tea at a riverside place, this is the Euphrates and the geography starts to feel ancient.

Back to Antep for dinner. Try yuvalama, the meatball soup with chickpeas that is harder to find now, only a few places make it well. Ask locals where they would eat it themselves.

Recommended experiences
Antep traditional cooking class Halfeti boat tour on the Euphrates
Day 6

Gaziantep to Izmir

Morning flight to Izmir via Istanbul, around four hours total with the connection. Try to take a flight that gets you into Izmir by lunchtime.

Base on the Kordon, the seaside promenade. The hotels along the front face west, the sun sets over the bay, the air cools by seven and the city comes alive.

Late afternoon, walk the Kemeraltı bazaar, the market in central Izmir that runs into itself like a working Levantine souk. Eat boyoz, the Sephardic pastry that is unique to Izmir, at a small bakery. The recipe came with the Sephardic Jews who fled Spain in 1492 and settled here, and the city kept the pastry.

Dinner along the Kordon. Aegean food is built on olive oil and herbs, lighter than what you ate in Antep. Try a zeytinyağlı place, the cold olive oil dishes that fill the meze case, particularly stuffed vine leaves and braised seasonal greens.

Day 7

Izmir, Olive Oil Country

Day trip north to the olive oil country around Bergama. The hills here have been pressing oil since antiquity. Several modern producers run tasting rooms with proper cupping format, you taste cold pressed monovarietals from local trees, learn to identify the bitterness and pepper that mark fresh oil.

Lunch in a village restaurant in the hills. Aegean food at its source, simple grilled fish, herb pies, olive oil with everything.

Afternoon back at Pergamon if you want ruins. The ancient city sits on a hill above modern Bergama, the acropolis has Roman and Hellenistic remains and a steep cable car up. Or skip it and head back to Izmir for a long Kordon evening.

Recommended experiences
Olive oil tasting tour from Izmir Pergamon archaeological site
Day 8

Izmir, Alaçatı and Çeşme

Drive west to Alaçatı, an Ottoman Greek village an hour from the city centre. The cobbled streets are full of stone houses with bright shutters, restored as hotels and restaurants. The food here is built around otlu, the dozens of edible wild herbs that grow on the peninsula. Order a herb mix at any of the meze places, with olive oil and lemon.

Lunch at Köşebaşı Alaçatı or one of the smaller meze houses. Levrek the Aegean sea bass at lunchtime is the move.

Afternoon, swim at Ilıca beach in Çeşme, the famous one with thermal springs running into the sea. Or pick a quieter cove if the crowd is not your thing. Fafa beach is nice if you have a car.

Back to Alaçatı for dinner. The town is at its best in the evening light when the day visitors leave.

Alaçatı food and wine tour Çeşme beaches day
Day 9

Izmir, Ephesus and Şirince

Day trip south to Ephesus, the largest Roman city ruin in the eastern Mediterranean. An hour and a half from Izmir. Get there early before the cruise ship buses, which start arriving around ten. Three hours minimum inside.

Lunch in Şirince, the wine village in the hills above Ephesus. A Greek village abandoned in 1923 and then partially repopulated, now full of wineries and family run restaurants. The fruit wines here range from genuinely good to very strange, taste before you buy.

Afternoon, drive back along the coast to Izmir. If you want one more stop, Sığacık is a small marina town an hour west of Izmir with a Saturday morning market that is one of the best in the region. If today is not Saturday, skip it.

Last dinner in Izmir. A meyhane on the Kordon, slow rakı, watch the lights come on across the bay.

Recommended experiences
Ephesus and Şirince day tour Şirince winery tasting
Day 10

Izmir, Departure

Long breakfast on the Kordon, walk the waterfront one more time. Izmir airport flies direct to most European capitals and several Middle Eastern hubs.

If you have time, the Izmir Agora, the Greek and Roman market in the city centre, is a quick visit and on the way to the airport. About forty five minutes.

General Notes

Flights. Two domestic flights, Istanbul to Antep, Antep to Izmir via Istanbul. Booked early, both legs together run around 100 to 150 EUR. Same day prices triple. Antep flies less frequently than the major cities, plan around the schedule.

Where to stay. Karaköy or Galata in Istanbul for the food. Bey Mahallesi in Antep, the restored stone house hotels around the old market. The Kordon in Izmir, or Alaçatı for a quieter base if you want a different mood.

Eating culture. Lunch is the bigger meal in Antep, dinner heavier in Istanbul and Izmir. Plan around when restaurants serve their best dishes, several Antep specialities like beyran and yuvalama are traditionally morning food and stop being served by noon. Ask hotels or guides for the right time slot.

Drinks. Rakı with water in Istanbul and Izmir meyhanes. Menengiç coffee in Antep, the wild pistachio infusion that is unique to the region. Çay with everything everywhere, free with breakfast, accept it.

Pace. Ten days is enough time to eat properly without rushing. If you cut to seven, drop Izmir and stay longer in Istanbul. If you extend, add Adana for kebabs and Hatay for the Arab Christian food culture along the Syrian border.
