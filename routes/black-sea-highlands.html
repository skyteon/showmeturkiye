Curated Route · 06

Black Sea Highlands

Duration7 days Best SeasonJune to early September BudgetMid range PaceSlow

The eastern Black Sea is the wettest, greenest part of Türkiye, and the part that surprises Turks themselves. Tea fields run up the mountains, mist sits in the valleys, and the road from Trabzon to Artvin passes through some of the country's most striking landscapes. Sümela Monastery cut into a cliff face above a valley, the Ayder plateau with wooden chalets and grazing cows, the Hemşin valleys where the Hemşinli people speak a dialect of Armenian and bake their bread in stone hot pots. Seven days at a slow pace, with cheese workshops, fog walks, and rivers cold enough to wake you up. This is the route for travellers who want quiet.

Day 1

Trabzon, Old City

Land in Trabzon, the historical capital of the eastern Black Sea coast. Once the seat of the Trabzon Empire, a Byzantine successor state that lasted into the fifteenth century. The city today is a working port, busier than it looks in photographs, with a small old core worth a half day.

Settle in centrally, walk to the Trabzon Hagia Sophia. Not the Istanbul one. This is a thirteenth century Byzantine church on a hillside above the sea, with original frescoes still visible inside, smaller and more intimate than its famous namesake. About an hour.

Walk down to the Atatürk Mansion for the late afternoon. The white wooden mansion was built by a Greek banker in 1903 and given to Atatürk by the city in 1937. The gardens look out over the sea, the wood smells like the centuries it has stood through.

Dinner in town. Try akçaabat köfte, the spiced lamb meatballs from the Akçaabat district that are the city's most famous dish. Akçaabat Köftecisi on the coast road is the canonical place.

Day 2

Trabzon, Sümela and Highlands

Drive south to Sümela Monastery, the Greek Orthodox monastery cut into a cliff face above the Altındere valley. Around 45 kilometres from Trabzon, an hour's drive. The monastery dates to the fourth century in legend, the standing buildings to the thirteenth. The frescoes inside are partial but striking. Allow two hours including the walk up from the parking area.

Lunch in Maçka at one of the trout restaurants along the river. Alabalık the local trout is farmed in pools fed by mountain streams. Order it grilled or steamed, the texture is firmer than supermarket trout.

Afternoon, drive higher to the Uzungöl lake. A glacial lake at 1100 metres, surrounded by tea houses on stilts and small wooden chalets. Touristy in summer, quiet in shoulder season. The walk around the lake takes thirty minutes, the views are best in the late afternoon when mist starts to settle.

Stay in Uzungöl tonight, in one of the wooden chalets above the water.

Day 3

Trabzon to Rize and Ayder

Drive east along the coast to Rize, around an hour. This is tea country. Rize province produces nearly all of Türkiye's tea, the steep coastal hills are covered in plantations that climb almost to the snowline.

Visit a tea garden, with pickers in season working the slopes. Several offer tasting tours where you learn the difference between bardak çayı, the dark steeped Turkish style, and lighter brews from the same leaves. The Çay Müzesi in Rize centre is small but worth thirty minutes.

Lunch in Rize, then drive south up the Fırtına Valley to Ayder Yaylası, the most famous of the highland plateaus. Around two hours of climbing through forest and valley, the road follows the Fırtına Çayı, the Storm River, named for its cold mountain runoff.

Ayder sits at 1300 metres, the air is thinner and cooler than the coast. Wooden chalets, grazing cows, hot springs feeding bathhouses, this is the canonical highland stay. Settle in, take the hot bath if you want, dinner in your hotel.

Day 4

Rize, Hemşin Valleys

Drive deeper into the Hemşin valleys, the side valleys above Ayder where the Hemşinli people live. Pull off in Çamlıhemşin, the main village, for late morning. The Hemşinli speak a dialect of Armenian and bake bread in stone hot pots called muhlama tava over open fires. Several restaurants here will do the bread to order.

Lunch is the local muhlama, a melted cheese and butter dish made with cornmeal that is the canonical Black Sea highland food. Dense, rich, the kind of thing that makes sense after a morning of cold mountain air.

Afternoon, walk up to the Pokut or Sal highland plateaus, both above 2000 metres. The road continues, but in summer mist tends to descend quickly and the plateaus disappear. If conditions are clear, these are the most photographed highland views in Türkiye, wooden houses scattered across grass slopes with peaks behind.

Back to Ayder for the evening, or stay in a Hemşin guesthouse if you find one. Tomorrow is a long drive.

Day 5

Rize to Artvin via Coast

Drive east along the coast to Hopa, then inland to Artvin, around four hours total. The road clings to the coast for most of the way, with steep green hills above and the sea below.

Lunch in Hopa or one of the small coastal towns. The Black Sea here has a different character from the Aegean, colder, denser, less postcard. Hamsi, the Black Sea anchovy, is the regional fish, in season from November to March, otherwise other small fish substitute.

Afternoon climb into Artvin province. The road switches back up the mountain, gaining altitude through forest. Artvin city itself is built into a slope, with neighbourhoods stacked vertically and a single main road that snakes through.

Stay in Artvin centre or drive on to Borçka for a quieter base. Tonight is your gateway to the Macahel valleys tomorrow.

Day 6

Artvin, Macahel and Borders

Drive into the Macahel valleys, the chain of villages along the Georgian border that sit in some of the densest forest in Türkiye. The road from Borçka winds for an hour up to Camili, the largest of the Macahel villages. This is officially a UNESCO biosphere reserve, with primary forest still standing in places.

Walk to the Camili Bridge, the wooden Ottoman bridge that arcs over the river. The valley here is genuinely off the standard tourist map, fewer than 1500 people live in the entire Macahel system, the way of life is built around bees, honey, and small scale forestry.

Lunch in a village house if you can arrange it through your hotel. The Macahel honey is dark, mountain forest honey, distinctive and worth taking home if you have luggage room.

Afternoon return to Artvin, evening in town. Tomorrow you start back.

Day 7

Artvin to Trabzon, Departure

Drive back to Trabzon, around five hours. This is a transit day but the coast is worth seeing one more time on the way west, the geography reversing itself.

Stop in Of or one of the small coast towns for late lunch. Hamsili pilav, anchovies layered over rice, is the regional dish if hamsi is in season.

Trabzon airport is near the coast, twenty minutes from the city centre. Direct flights to Istanbul, Ankara, Izmir, plus a few European destinations.

If your flight is late, walk the Trabzon waterfront one more time before leaving.

General Notes

Driving. All driving is on good roads. Trabzon to Rize is an hour, Rize to Ayder two hours uphill, Ayder to Artvin four hours via the coast. A rental car is the right choice for this route, public buses run but adds days. Roads to highland plateaus turn unpaved beyond Ayder, four wheel drive helps after rain.

Where to stay. Trabzon centre or Akçaabat for the city. Wooden chalet in Uzungöl. Highland chalet in Ayder, ideally with a wood stove. Artvin city centre or Borçka for proximity to Macahel.

Season. June to early September. The mountain plateaus are only accessible by road from late May to October, snow closes them otherwise. July and August are warmest but mist is more frequent. Late June and early September give the best balance.

Food specifics. The Black Sea kitchen is butter, corn, hamsi, and mountain greens. Try kuymak the cornmeal cheese fondue, laz böreği the custard pastry, karalahana the local black cabbage soup. Tea is mandatory and free with everything.

Pace. Seven days at this pace is the right shape for the region. Cutting to five means dropping Artvin, which is the quietest and possibly the most rewarding stop. Adding more time, the route can extend west toward Sinop and the lesser known central Black Sea coast.
