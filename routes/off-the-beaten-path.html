Curated Route · 10

Off the Beaten Path

Duration12 days Best SeasonJune to early September BudgetMid range PaceSlow

For experienced travellers who already saw the postcards. Twelve days through three regions most foreign visitors miss, and most Turks themselves have not done. Kastamonu for wooden Ottoman houses and Yörük mountain villages along the central Black Sea. Artvin for near virgin forests and the Macahel valleys against the Georgian border. Hakkari for the Kurdish highlands deep in the southeast, where time genuinely slows and the geography turns Caucasian. The pace is slow on purpose, the comfort is moderate, the photographs are unlike anything in the brochures. This is the version of Türkiye that surprises Turks themselves.

Day 1

Arrival in Kastamonu

Fly into Ankara, drive north to Kastamonu, around three hours. Or fly direct to Kastamonu airport, smaller and less frequent, but on the days the flight runs it saves the drive.

Settle in. The city sits in a valley with wooden Ottoman houses climbing the slopes, several restored as boutique hotels in the old quarter near Cebrail Mahallesi. The streets here are narrow, cobbled, and quiet. The whole city moves at a different speed than coastal Türkiye.

Late afternoon, walk to the Kastamonu Castle, the Byzantine and Ottoman fortress on the hill above the old town. The climb is fifteen minutes, the views over the wooden roof tiles below are the photograph the city sells itself on.

Dinner in town, a small place in the old quarter. Try etli ekmek, the long flatbread topped with spiced lamb, served everywhere here, and banduma, the local layered chicken and bread dish unique to the region.

Day 2

Kastamonu, Safranbolu Day Trip

Drive south to Safranbolu, around two hours. This is a UNESCO World Heritage town, the most preserved Ottoman wooden architecture in Türkiye. The whole old town is essentially a museum, but a working one with families still living in the houses and shops still trading from spaces that were here three hundred years ago.

Walk the Çarşı, the historic market street, then up to the Hıdırlık Hill for the photograph that sells the town, wooden roofs cascading down the slope with the river below. The light is best mid morning when the sun crosses east to west across the valley.

Lunch at one of the historic han, the restored Ottoman caravanserais turned restaurants. Try safran, the saffron the town is named for, used in local desserts and rice dishes.

Afternoon, drive back to Kastamonu via the longer scenic road through forest. Pınarbaşı village along the way has a small lake and the cleanest air outside the highlands. Brief stop, twenty minutes.

Evening back in Kastamonu, dinner at the hotel.

Day 3

Kastamonu, Yörük Villages

Drive into the Ilgaz Mountains south of Kastamonu, around an hour. The range here is forest covered, with small Yörük villages, the seasonal nomadic communities that historically moved sheep between summer and winter pastures. Several still operate this way, though most are now permanently settled.

Pull off in one of the villages along the road, Çatalzeytin or Kambur. The wooden houses here are smaller and older than in Safranbolu, less restored, more lived in. Talk to a family if invited, this is genuinely lived in landscape rather than heritage tourism.

Lunch in a village house if you can arrange through your hotel, otherwise back to Kastamonu. Try tirit, the lamb broth and bread soup that is winter food in these villages.

Afternoon, the Mahmut Bey Mosque in Kasaba, around 45 minutes from Kastamonu centre. A fourteenth century wooden mosque with original carved interior columns and ceiling, built without a single iron nail. One of the oldest wooden mosques in Türkiye and probably the most beautiful. Allow an hour.

Back to Kastamonu, last evening in town.

Day 4

Kastamonu to Trabzon

Long drive east along the Black Sea coast to Trabzon, around eight hours total. This is a transit day but the road follows the coast for most of the way, with steep green hills above and the sea below.

Break the drive in Sinop, around three hours from Kastamonu, the northernmost point of Türkiye. Walk the old castle walls along the harbour, lunch at one of the small fish restaurants on the water. The town itself is sleepy and pleasant, worth two hours.

Continue along the coast through Samsun, Ordu, Giresun. The coast road is busy in places but the scenery is consistent, mountains and sea, hazelnut and tea plantations climbing the slopes.

Arrive Trabzon late afternoon. Settle in centrally, dinner along the seafront.

Day 5

Trabzon to Artvin via Coast

Drive east along the coast to Hopa, then inland to Artvin, around four hours total. The road clings to the coast, you watch the geography climb gradually as you go.

Lunch in Hopa or Arhavi, one of the small coastal towns. The Black Sea here is colder than the Aegean, denser in colour, less postcard. Try fresh fish if it is in season, otherwise the standard kebab and pide.

Climb into Artvin province in the afternoon. The road switches back up the mountain, gaining altitude through dense forest. Artvin city itself is built into a slope, neighbourhoods stacked vertically, a single main road snaking through.

Stay in Artvin centre or in Borçka for a quieter base. Tonight is your gateway to the Macahel valleys tomorrow.

Day 6

Artvin, Macahel and Forest

Drive into the Macahel valleys, the chain of villages along the Georgian border in some of the densest forest in Türkiye. The road from Borçka winds for an hour up to Camili, the largest Macahel village. This is officially a UNESCO biosphere reserve, with primary forest still standing in places.

Walk to the Camili Bridge, the wooden Ottoman arch over the river. The valley here is genuinely off the standard tourist map, fewer than 1500 people live in the entire Macahel system, the way of life is built around bees, honey, and small scale forestry.

Lunch at a village house if you can arrange it. The Macahel honey is dark, mountain forest honey, distinctive and worth taking home. The mountain butter and aged cheese are also local specialities.

Afternoon hike one of the forest trails near Camili. A few villages still keep traditional kara koyun bee colonies in hollow logs hung from trees, an ancient method that produces the deepest forest honey.

Back to Artvin or Borçka for the evening.

Day 7

Artvin to Erzurum via Yusufeli

Drive south through Yusufeli, the river town along the Çoruh, around two hours. The Çoruh is one of the wildest rivers in Türkiye, traditionally famous for white water rafting, though recent dam construction has changed long stretches.

Lunch in Yusufeli, then continue south toward Erzurum, around three more hours. The drive crosses high passes, the geography turns from forest to alpine plateau and then to the high steppe of eastern Anatolia. Watch the elevation, you climb to 2000 metres at the highest pass.

Arrive Erzurum late afternoon. The city sits at 1900 metres, the air is thin, the architecture turns from coastal wood to inland stone. Walk the Çifte Minareli Medrese in the old town, a thirteenth century Seljuk theological school with twin minarets carved with intricate Seljuk patterns.

Dinner. Try cağ kebabı, the horizontal lamb skewer cooked in front of a flame, traditionally served with sliced onion and tomato. The dish is local to Erzurum and Erzincan.

Day 8

Erzurum to Hakkari, Long Drive

Long drive south to Hakkari, around eight hours. This is the longest single transit day of the route. The road goes through Van, the lake city, and then climbs into the southeastern mountains.

Break the drive in Van for late lunch, around four hours from Erzurum. The lake here is the largest in Türkiye, salty, with a unique fish called inci kefali the pearl mullet that runs up the rivers in spring. Akdamar Island in Lake Van has a tenth century Armenian church with restored frescoes, a 30 minute boat ride from the southern shore.

Continue south to Hakkari in the afternoon, around four more hours. The geography turns properly Caucasian here, snow capped peaks above the road most of the year, deep valleys, very few villages.

Arrive Hakkari late evening. Hakkari is small and remote, hotels are simple, the food is Kurdish kitchen, hearty lamb and yoghurt dishes. This is genuinely the edge of the country.

Day 9

Hakkari, Mountain Day

Drive into the Cilo Sat mountains, the range north of Hakkari that contains some of the highest peaks in Türkiye outside Ararat. The road climbs to one of the high alpine plateaus, around 2500 metres. Snow lies on the summits well into July.

Walk one of the lake circuits if you have a guide, the area requires permits and local knowledge in spots. Cilo glacial lakes are the destination if you can reach them, alpine pools at 3000 metres surrounded by snow even in summer. This is genuinely Caucasian wilderness.

Lunch in a village along the way, simple food, lamb and rice, yoghurt and cheese. The Kurdish hospitality here is real, you will be offered tea repeatedly and expected to drink it.

Back to Hakkari for evening. Last dinner in the southeast.

Day 10

Hakkari to Mardin

Drive west to Mardin, around six hours. The route descends from the mountains into the Mesopotamian plain, the geography flattens and warms, the air gets drier.

Stop in Midyat for lunch, two hours before Mardin. Smaller, quieter than Mardin, also stone built, with several active Syriac neighbourhoods and silver workshops where filigree silver is still made by hand.

Continue to Mardin in the afternoon. After the rough overnight stops in Hakkari and Erzurum, the comfort of a stone house hotel here will register. Stay in the old town facing south.

Sunset from a rooftop terrace, the views southward over the Mesopotamian plain are why people fall in love with Mardin. Dinner on the terrace.

Day 11

Mardin, Recovery Day

Slow morning. After ten days of long drives and rough places, this is a recovery day. Walk the Mardin old town from west to east at your own pace, the side alleys hold the best frames.

Lunch at a Süryani place. Try kibe and harire, the Syriac specialities that are unique to this region.

Afternoon, optional visit to Deyrulzafaran Monastery, fifteen minutes outside town. A fifth century Syriac Orthodox monastery still active, the home base of the Patriarch until the early twentieth century.

Evening, last dinner of the trip on a Mardin terrace. The geography has gone full circle, from coastal wooden houses to mountain forests to high steppe to limestone cliffs over Mesopotamia.

Day 12

Mardin, Departure

Slow morning. If your flight is late, walk the old town one more time as the morning light arrives.

Mardin airport is thirty minutes from the centre, with direct flights to Istanbul and Ankara. Connections to Europe via Istanbul.

If you have time, the Mardin Museum in the city centre holds Mesopotamian artefacts and Syriac manuscripts gathered over the centuries. Worth an hour.

General Notes

Driving and logistics. This is a driving heavy route. A four wheel drive rental is worth the upgrade for the Macahel and Cilo legs, both involve unpaved roads. Total driving across the route is roughly 2500 kilometres, broken across several days. Local guides are recommended for Hakkari and Macahel, partly for navigation and partly for Kurdish or Hemşin language access where Turkish is the second language.

Where to stay. Restored boutique in Kastamonu old town. Coastal hotel in Trabzon. Artvin centre or Borçka for Macahel access. Erzurum centre, simple. Hakkari is genuinely remote, accept that hotels are basic. Mardin old town stone house at the end is the comfort recovery.

Permits and access. Macahel and Cilo regions require notification for foreign travellers, your hotel or local guide will handle this. Some border zones near Georgia and Iran require special permits, again handled locally. Don't skip this, the gendarmerie in these areas does check.

Season. Late June to early September. The northern route through Macahel and the Cilo mountains is only accessible by road in summer, snow closes them otherwise. Mardin and the southeast can be done year round, but the through route requires the summer window.

Pace and difficulty. This is genuinely a hard route. Long drives, simple accommodations in places, food and language differences as you cross between regions. Twelve days is the minimum, fifteen would be more comfortable. Travellers who want a softer version should drop the Hakkari leg and replace with two more days in Macahel or Mardin.
