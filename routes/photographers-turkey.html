Curated Route · 08

Photographer's Türkiye

Duration10 days Best SeasonApril to May, September to October BudgetMid range PaceBalanced

Built around four distinct visual languages. Cappadocia for volcanic valleys at sunrise with balloons. Pamukkale for white travertine pools, especially the half hour after dawn before the buses arrive. Mardin for the ochre stone city overlooking the Mesopotamian plain, best in late afternoon when the limestone catches the side light. Muğla for the turquoise coves of the Lycian coast, drone friendly and best from the water. Ten days lets you wait for weather and light, which is the difference between snapshots and photographs. The pace alternates active shooting days with rest, because shooting hard for ten days straight produces worse work than shooting well for six.

Day 1

Cappadocia, Settling In

Fly into Nevşehir via Istanbul, transfer to Göreme. Cave hotel with a south facing terrace if you can manage it, the breakfast view is itself a worthwhile setup.

Reserve a balloon flight for tomorrow morning, also a sunrise viewing slot for the next day. Operators sell out and the day starts at four.

Late afternoon, scout Sunset Point and Lover's Hill as locations for tomorrow's land based dawn shooting. Sunset tonight from one of these, the light at this hour gives you a reading on the next morning's east light.

Dinner early, sleep early. Tomorrow is a four AM start.

Day 2

Cappadocia, Balloon Sunrise

Up at four. Pickup at five. You are in a balloon for sunrise on Day Two of the trip rather than Day Three, because dawn balloon photography from the air takes practice and you want a second attempt on the ground tomorrow.

Shoot from the basket. Standard balloon rides bring around 80 other balloons in the air at peak season, you photograph them rather than yourself. The basket gives you full 360 horizon, and at altitude you can frame valleys with balloons over them. Wide lenses for landscapes, longer for individual baskets backlit by the sun.

Back to the hotel by nine for breakfast. Sleep through midday.

Late afternoon, walk Rose Valley with the late light. The rock turns rose pink for about an hour before sunset, and the trail through the valley has carved church openings every few hundred metres. Two hours of walking, multiple compositions per kilometre.

Day 3

Cappadocia, Land Sunrise and Underground

Up at four thirty again. This time you shoot from Lover's Hill or Sunset Point, watching the same balloon launches you were inside yesterday. Tripods, low ISO, long exposures during the blue hour, then bracketing as the light arrives. The lift off period at sunrise is roughly 25 minutes wide.

Back to the hotel for breakfast and rest.

Afternoon, drive to one of the underground cities, Derinkuyu or Kaymaklı. The shooting here is technical, very low light, you need a sturdy tripod and a wide enough lens to capture the chambers at proper distance. Fast prime in 24mm or 35mm range works best.

Evening at the hotel. Tomorrow is a moving day.

Day 4

Cappadocia to Pamukkale

Drive south to Pamukkale, around six hours through central Anatolia. Or fly via Istanbul to Denizli airport and rent a car, which is faster but breaks the road trip flow.

Arrive Pamukkale late afternoon. Stay in Pamukkale village rather than further out, the early morning trip up to the travertines is what matters and you want to be ten minutes from the entrance.

Walk up to the upper terrace for sunset, the light hits the white travertine sideways from the west and the shadows produce more depth than midday flat light. Bring shoes for the warm pool walking, the limestone is softer than concrete.

Dinner in the village, sleep early. Tomorrow is a five AM start.

Day 5

Pamukkale, Sunrise and Hierapolis

Up at five. Be at the gate when it opens, half hour before sunrise. The thirty minutes after dawn are the photograph, the white travertine catching first warm light with morning mist still rising from the warm pools. By eight the tour buses arrive and the moment is over.

Spend two hours shooting. The pools cascade down the hillside in stages, multiple compositions, the trick is finding clean foreground without footprints. Walk barefoot, leave gear bag in one place and move minimally, water reflections matter.

Late morning, walk the Hierapolis ruins above the pools. The Greco Roman city built around the springs has a Roman theatre still mostly intact, plus a kilometre long necropolis. The stone here goes warm grey in late morning sun. Two hours.

Lunch in the village, then drive to Karahayıt 5km north for the red travertines, much smaller and overlooked, but the iron oxide colour is worth a separate visit. Half an hour.

Evening, drive south toward Mardin, but the journey is long. Better to break it overnight in Adıyaman or Şanlıurfa and continue tomorrow.

Day 6

To Mardin via Şanlıurfa

Drive east to Mardin, around six hours from Pamukkale. Or fly Pamukkale to Mardin via Istanbul, an hour in the air plus the connection. The drive is genuinely beautiful through inland Anatolia, the flight is faster.

Arrive Mardin late afternoon. Old town stay in a stone house hotel facing south.

Mardin at sunset is the photograph the city is known for. The honey coloured limestone houses cascade down the south facing cliff, the Mesopotamian plain stretches out below for sixty kilometres, and the late light turns the entire city gold. Walk the main street from west to east as the sun drops, multiple compositions, every alley off the main is a frame.

Dinner on a rooftop terrace, the views southward at night with the village lights below are also worth a long lens.

Day 7

Mardin, Stone Studies

Morning walk, harder light, but the side alleys catch slot light off the white walls. The Sultan Isa Medresesi doorway is the most photographed spot in the old city for a reason, but the alleys behind hold dozens of equally good frames with no one in them.

Lunch on a rooftop, then afternoon at Deyrulzafaran Monastery fifteen minutes outside town. Yellow stone in afternoon light, courtyard arches, intricate stone carving on door frames. Modest dress required, no flash inside the chapel.

Back to Mardin for the second sunset, this time from a different vantage. The east end of the old city, near the Kasımiye Medresesi, gives you a different angle on the south slope.

Last dinner in Mardin, take it slow.

Day 8

Mardin to Muğla Coast

Fly Mardin to Dalaman or Antalya via Istanbul. A long travel day, six to eight hours total with the connection. Try to land in Dalaman by late afternoon.

Drive to Fethiye or directly to Ölüdeniz. Stay at a cove hotel with private beach access if you can, the morning swim shots from the room rather than from a public beach are part of why you book the upgrade.

Walk the Ölüdeniz beach at sunset. The blue lagoon's calm water and pine forest backdrop is the standard photograph but the strip of beach outside the lagoon often has cleaner foreground.

Confirm tomorrow's gulet day or paragliding session if you booked one.

Day 9

Muğla, Drone Day

On the water. Take a gulet through the Fethiye coves, the boat lets you reach beaches and ruins not accessible by road. Butterfly Valley, the Twelve Islands, the half submerged ruins of Tersane Island.

If you fly drones, this is the day. Most coastal areas around Fethiye allow drone flying outside the air corridors and military zones. Confirm with your captain. Drone shots of the gulet from above, anchored in turquoise water, are the photograph the route makes possible.

Lunch on the boat. Late afternoon return to the marina.

Evening, drive up to Babadağ for sunset. The mountain above Ölüdeniz, the same one paragliders launch from, has views over the entire Lycian coast. Cable car runs into the late afternoon. Drone or telephoto from the summit, the coves below catching last light.

Day 10

Departure

If your flight is late, drive to Kayaköy, the abandoned Greek village above Fethiye for a final morning shoot. The empty stone houses with morning light hitting the slope from the east is the canonical Kayaköy frame.

Or, if you are saturated and want one quiet morning, the harbour at Fethiye in soft light is photograph enough.

Dalaman airport is forty minutes from Fethiye centre. Flights direct to most European capitals.

General Notes

Flights and route logistics. Five domestic flights total in this route, Istanbul to Nevşehir, Nevşehir to Pamukkale or drive, Pamukkale to Mardin or drive, Mardin to Dalaman, Dalaman home. Booking flights flexibly with date holding helps when weather forecasts shift the shooting schedule.

Equipment notes. A medium tripod, fast wide prime in 24 to 35mm range, a longer telezoom for compressing distant balloons or village shots, and a drone if you fly. ND filters help in Pamukkale midday. The volcanic dust in Cappadocia is hard on gear, bring lens cleaning supplies.

Light and weather. Cappadocia balloons fly only when weather permits, you can lose mornings to wind. Build in flexibility, three nights gives you two real attempts. Pamukkale needs clear morning light to read its colour, overcast days produce flat results. Mardin is best in late afternoon when the sun side lights the south facing cliff. The coast is shootable any clear day.

Permits and access. Drone use requires registration with Turkish authorities for non residents, the SHGM directorate handles this online. Without registration, drones are confiscated. Most main attractions allow tripods, exceptions are inside religious buildings and the Hierapolis museum.

Pace. Ten days is built for shooting properly. If you cut to seven, drop Mardin, which is the longest detour and the most weather sensitive light. If you extend, add Sumela Monastery on the Black Sea coast or the Lycian rock tombs near Antalya.
