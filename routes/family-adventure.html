Curated Route · 11

Family Adventure

Duration9 days Best SeasonApril to June, September to October BudgetMid range PaceBalanced

A nine day plan built around what kids will actually remember and enjoy. Istanbul's Bosphorus boats and the Spice Bazaar smells. Cappadocia's underground cities, balloon flights for older kids, and ATV tours through the valleys. Antalya's beaches and the Aspendos theatre big enough to make any kid feel small. Pamukkale's white travertine pools, which kids tend to love and which are warm enough to swim in. The pace alternates active days with rest days because no one travels well exhausted, and the activities are chosen for the moment of wonder rather than the educational checklist. The hotels along the way are kid friendly, with pools and family rooms, but not the giant resort kind that feel like airports.

Day 1

Istanbul, Old City Walking

Land in Istanbul, settle in Sultanahmet with a hotel that has family rooms or interconnecting options. The historic peninsula is car free in places and walkable, which is the pace kids need on the first day.

Late afternoon, visit the Basilica Cistern. The underground reservoir is the most kid friendly of the major Istanbul sites, dim, atmospheric, with carp swimming in the water and the Medusa head columns at the back. Allow forty five minutes.

Then walk over to the Hippodrome square between Hagia Sophia and the Blue Mosque. The Egyptian Obelisk and Serpent Column here are easy to absorb in fifteen minutes, and the open square gives kids space to move.

Dinner in Sultanahmet, kid friendly options include köfte houses, simple grilled meatballs that most children eat without protest. Sultanahmet Köftecisi on the main road is the canonical place.

Day 2

Istanbul, Bosphorus and Bazaars

Morning at the Spice Bazaar. Smaller than the Grand Bazaar and more sensory, kids tend to engage more here, vendors offer samples, the smells and colours are concentrated. Allow an hour with snack breaks.

Then walk down to Eminönü for a Bosphorus boat. The public ferry from Eminönü to Anadolu Kavağı is the cheap option, two hours each way with a few stops. Kids tend to enjoy the deck more than any sit down activity, especially the moment of crossing under the bridges.

Lunch on board or at the Anadolu Kavağı stop, fish at the small restaurants there. Back to Eminönü mid afternoon.

Late afternoon, walk to Galata Tower. The fourteenth century Genoese tower has an elevator now and a 360 view from the top, including over the rooftops of the old city back across the Golden Horn. Allow an hour.

Dinner in Karaköy along the waterfront.

Day 3

Istanbul to Cappadocia

Morning flight to Nevşehir or Kayseri, around an hour and a half. Both airports are forty five minutes from Göreme.

Stay in a cave hotel with family rooms. Most boutique cave hotels have larger family suites, the experience of staying in a room carved into volcanic rock is itself a memory for kids, especially younger ones.

Settle in. Late afternoon, walk the Göreme Open Air Museum, the rock cut Byzantine churches with painted interiors. About 90 minutes, kids tend to engage with the doorway sized openings and stair climbs. The Dark Church (Karanlık Kilise) has the best preserved frescoes and is a small additional ticket worth the climb.

Sunset at Sunset Point just above town. Reserve a balloon flight for tomorrow if your kids are old enough, most operators have age minimums of six to eight.

Day 4

Cappadocia, Balloons and Underground

If kids are old enough, the balloon flight at sunrise is the headline. Up at four thirty, in the air at sunrise. Most operators allow ages six and up, some only ten and up, confirm before booking. About an hour up, then a champagne landing in a field with a small ceremony.

Back to the hotel by nine for breakfast. If you skipped the balloon, sleep in and start the morning at the Avanos pottery workshop. The town along the river specialises in red clay pottery, several workshops will let kids try the wheel themselves for an extra fee. Hands on, messy, and memorable.

Lunch in Avanos or back in Göreme. Try testi kebabı, the lamb stew cooked sealed in a clay pot and broken open at the table. The breaking is the entertainment, the food itself is plain enough for most kids.

Afternoon at one of the underground cities, Kaymaklı is wider and easier than Derinkuyu for families. The chambers are connected by narrow tunnels and stairs, the engagement level is high, claustrophobic kids will struggle but most love it. Allow 90 minutes inside.

Evening back at the hotel, or for a treat an ATV tour through the valleys, available for older kids and teens, full safety equipment provided.

Day 5

Cappadocia to Pamukkale

Morning flight to Denizli via Istanbul, or drive south, around six hours. Most families fly to save time and fatigue.

Settle in Pamukkale village, a small town directly below the travertines. Hotels are simple but most have pools, useful in the afternoon heat.

Late afternoon, walk up to the travertines. The white limestone pools cascading down the hillside are made of calcium carbonate from hot mineral springs. The pools are warm, around 35 degrees, and walking shoeless is mandatory. Kids love them, both the wading and the strange landscape. The light is best in late afternoon when the sun crosses west to east across the slope.

Above the pools, Hierapolis is the Greco Roman city built around the springs. Most kids will engage with the Roman theatre, still mostly intact. The full ruins are too much for kids on a hot day, focus on the theatre and the necropolis entrance.

Dinner in the village, sleep early.

Day 6

Pamukkale to Antalya

Drive south to Antalya, around three hours. The road descends from the inland plateau down to the Mediterranean coast, the geography changes from steppe to pine forest to coast.

Stay in Kaleiçi, the old town inside the Roman walls. Several family hotels here have pools, gardens, and breakfast spaces that work for kids. Boutique enough to feel special without being chaos.

Settle in, swim if your hotel has a pool. Late afternoon, walk down to Kaleiçi marina, the harbour at the base of the cliff. The boats below the cliff and the old town walls behind are a setting kids enjoy without anyone having to explain anything.

Dinner along the harbour, fresh fish or köfte, both reliable kid food.

Day 7

Antalya, Aspendos and Beach

Morning trip to Aspendos, the Roman theatre from the second century AD that is still almost completely intact. Forty five minutes from Antalya. Kids respond strongly to the scale, the theatre seats 12,000 and you can sit in the original stone benches and shout to test the acoustics, which still work.

Lunch on the way back, then beach for the afternoon. Konyaaltı is the long city beach with calmer water and lifeguards. Lara on the eastern side is sandier but busier. Both work for families, choose by what the morning has been like.

Late afternoon ice cream and swimming. The water is warm enough to swim in from May to October.

Dinner back in Kaleiçi, the night markets and street food in the old town are a softer evening for tired kids.

Day 8

Antalya, Düden Falls and Aquarium

Morning at Düden Waterfalls, two falls along the Düden River, one inland and one falling directly into the sea. The seaside falls can be seen from a boat tour or from the cliff above, kids respond to both. The boat option is more memorable. Allow two hours.

Lunch in town. If kids need a wind down, an aquarium visit, the Antalya Aquarium has a long underwater tunnel and a varied collection. Two to three hours, and air conditioned which matters in summer.

Afternoon back at the beach or pool, depending on energy. Last evening in the old town.

Dinner with a view, the clifftop restaurants above Yat Limanı have the longest light and a view kids will hold in mind for a while.

Day 9

Antalya, Departure

Slow morning. If your flight is late, one more swim, breakfast in the old town, last walk along the marina.

Antalya airport is twenty minutes from Kaleiçi, with direct flights to most European capitals.

General Notes

Flights. Two domestic flights, Istanbul to Cappadocia, Cappadocia to Denizli (or Antalya). Booked early, the total runs around 100 to 180 EUR per person. Pegasus tends to be cheaper than Turkish Airlines for families with luggage.

Where to stay. Sultanahmet boutique in Istanbul with family rooms. Cave hotel in Göreme with family suites, choose by terrace and pool. Pamukkale village hotel with pool. Kaleiçi old town boutique in Antalya with garden and pool.

Pace. This is the pace that works for families with school age children. With younger kids, drop Pamukkale and add an extra day in either Antalya or Cappadocia. With teenagers, you can run the route faster and add another stop, Bodrum on the Aegean works well as an extension.

Practical kid tips. Pack hats, sunscreen, swim shoes for Pamukkale and rocky beaches, refillable water bottles. Most museums and sites are free for kids under six, half price under twelve. Most family rooms in Türkiye are two double beds in one room, request twins if needed.

Food. Turkish food is generally kid friendly, plain grilled meats and breads work for most. Avoid the spicier southeast specialities for younger kids. Restaurants are universally welcoming, no need to seek out kid menus, simple grilled fish or köfte is everywhere.
