Trabzon: Why First-Time Visitors Always Come Back

A monastery carved into a cliff, a tea glass at dawn over the city, and a köfte that makes every other köfte feel incomplete. Trabzon earns its second visit before the first one ends.

By Halil BekarApril 20269 min read
Scroll

The first question is always the same: why Trabzon? The answer is both simple and impossible to explain from a distance.

This is a city where the humidity works its way into everything, the smell of tea is inseparable from the smell of the Black Sea, and the grey the water turns each morning is a different grey from the morning before. You cannot photograph these things. You can only be inside them. That is why people who go once tend to find reasons to go again.

01
Byzantine Cliff

Sümela Monastery

At the canyon face, before the crowd arrives

Sümela was founded in 386 AD on a vertical cliff face in the Altındere valley, a location that seems chosen specifically to make construction impossible. The monks managed anyway, and for sixteen centuries the monastery has been clinging to that rock regardless of who controlled the territory below it. Byzantine emperors, Comnenian dynasty, Ottoman sultans, the modern Turkish republic. Sümela outlasted the logic of all of them.

The standard visit arrives by tour bus at 10am when the path is full and the entrance queue is long. The correct visit arrives at eight o'clock, before the monastery opens, and waits on the steps of the canyon below. At that hour the Altındere valley still has mist sitting between the trees and the cliff, and the building materialises out of it slowly as the morning burns through. That image is the reason to come to Trabzon.

Sümela has outlasted Byzantine emperors, Ottoman sultans, and three centuries of abandonment. It will outlast the tour groups too.

Photographer's Note

Arrive before 8am. The mist in the valley is gone by 9:30. The cliff face catches morning light from the east for roughly 45 minutes. That is the window. Everything else is just documentation.

02
Highland Lake

Uzungöl

What the postcards skip

Uzungöl is genuinely beautiful. The lake sits in a valley of tea and spruce, the water is still in the early morning, and the surrounding ridges have a graduated green that changes register as the altitude rises. The problem is that this is also the most photographed location in the eastern Black Sea region, and on a July weekend it shows.

The way to experience Uzungöl correctly is to ignore the lake's immediate perimeter and walk the hillside paths above the valley. Fifteen minutes uphill from the main road you leave the shops and reach tea gardens run out of farmhouses, where the owner's grandmother will almost certainly insist on refilling your glass twice without asking. The view from those gardens is the same valley, minus the crowd. That is the version worth coming for.

Photographer's Note

Winter morning at Uzungöl: fog sits on the water, the surrounding hills are snow-covered, and the valley holds the silence of a place that has turned its back on its own tourist reputation. October through December are the honest months here.

03
City Ritual

Boztepe

A morning over the city

Boztepe is a hill above Trabzon where the city's tea gardens face the sea. There is nothing dramatic about the act of going there. You sit down, someone brings tea, you look at the city below and the Black Sea beyond it. That is all.

What makes it matter is time. Come at seven in the morning when the tea gardens are mostly empty and the city is just beginning to make its sounds. The harbour below, the old walls, the minarets, the industrial cranes, the sea. Trabzon is not a city that asks to be aestheticised. At Boztepe it simply presents itself and lets you decide what to make of it. That honest quality is the thing most worth keeping from a visit here.

04
Black Sea Table

What to Eat in Trabzon

Köfte, hamsi, and the corn bread question

Akçaabat köfte is made with lamb and beef combined, formed without egg or breadcrumb, grilled directly without fat added. It arrives grey-brown and unpretentious. One bite explains the regional loyalty. The version you find in Akçaabat district, made by establishments that have been doing it since before you were born, is measurably different from any other version of köfte on the country. Eat it with nothing but bread and raw onion.

Hamsi season runs October to February. During those months the anchovy is available fried, steamed, baked into rice, folded into bread. The hamsi pilav, anchovies laid over rice and baked, is the dish that makes the season make sense. Outside those months it exists, but it is not the same fish.

Corn bread, mısır ekmeği, is what the breakfast table is organised around in the Black Sea highlands. Dense, slightly sweet, best with a hard local cheese and unsalted butter. The version that comes out of a wood-fired stone oven in a village above Trabzon at seven in the morning is its own category of experience.

05
Planning

When to Visit Trabzon

Spring, autumn, and the honest case for winter

May is ideal. The highland meadows have opened, tea gardens are in their first flush, the air is cool and everything is the particular green that only exists in the eastern Black Sea region for six weeks of the year. October runs a close second: quieter, the hazelnut harvest finished, the city resettling into its own pace.

Winter makes a genuine argument. The city does not shut down. The upper neighbourhoods take snow, Boztepe acquires a different quality of silence, and the off-season restaurants are where the actual Trabzon eating happens. If cold does not disqualify a destination for you, January or February here is not a consolation prize.
