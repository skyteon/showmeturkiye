Balıkesir: The Province Where the Aegean Begins

Kazdağları rises above Homer's Ida Mountain on one side and Ayvalık faces a sea full of islands on the other. Between them, a province that most visitors drive through on the way somewhere more famous.

By Show Me Türkiye TeamApril 20268 min read
Scroll

The road from Istanbul south follows the coast and most cars accelerate through Balıkesir without stopping. This is a reasonable navigation decision that misses two things worth missing a few hours of driving for.

Kazdağları is a mountain range that carries the weight of being both a national park and the place where Greek mythology located events of some consequence. Ayvalık is a coastal town of Ottoman-Greek architecture and Aegean light where the olive oil culture of the region reaches its most concentrated form. Neither of these is on the mainstream Türkiye itinerary. Both are within easy reach of anything that is.

01
Mythological Mountain

Kazdağları — Mount Ida

Where the Trojan War's first argument was made

Kazdağları is the modern name for ancient Mount Ida, which Greek mythology identifies as the site where Paris awarded the golden apple to Aphrodite, setting in motion the events that produced the Trojan War. The mountain's role in the ancient imagination is legible in its landscape: it dominates the Troad plain toward Troy, it is visible from the sea, and it catches clouds in a way that creates the particular atmospheric drama that makes mountains feel inhabited by something larger than themselves.

As a national park, Kazdağları is a working forest of black pine and oak. The trails are well-maintained, the springs on the slopes produce water cold enough to carry and drink directly, and the Pınarbaşı plateau at the heart of the park has a quality of stillness that the mountain's mythological reputation turns out to be appropriate for. The park's biodiversity is well-documented — it contains species of both Aegean and Anatolian flora within close vertical range.

Homer placed Zeus watching the Trojan War from Ida's summit. The mountain is still there, still watching the Troad plain. The war has been over for three thousand years.

Photographer's Note

Kazdağları sunrise from the Sazıkavak plateau: the mountain casts its shadow westward over the Aegean at first light, and the shadow line retreats as the sun rises. The forest smell at that altitude in the hour after dawn is distinctive enough to remember for years.

02
Aegean Town

Ayvalık

The olive oil coast at its most concentrated

Ayvalık is one of the most architecturally coherent small cities on the Aegean coast. The historic district consists of narrow streets of late Ottoman-Greek houses, many of them still occupied, many in various states of careful restoration, and a small number converted into boutique hotels and restaurants. The town was substantially built by the Rum Greek community before the 1923 population exchange, which means the architecture has a particular quality of attention to proportion that is visible even in the unremarkable buildings.

The waterfront faces west, which means the sunset from any point along the harbour is framed by the islands. Olive groves surround the town on all inland approaches. The morning market near the old harbour runs local produce through channels that have been operating the same way for generations. If your interest in Aegean food culture extends beyond the tourist-restaurant version, this is where it becomes something more honest.

Traveler's Note

The old tannery neighbourhood behind the market in Ayvalık has a concentration of small producers — olive oil, local cheese, honey, preserved vegetables — selling directly without retail markup. Go on a weekday morning. Bring a cooler.

03
Island

Cunda Island

Connected by causeway, separated by atmosphere

Cunda Island, also known as Alibey Adası, is connected to Ayvalık by a causeway and is functionally a separate experience despite the short distance. The island is smaller and quieter, the streets are narrower, and the sea is visible from more angles than on the mainland. The architecture is similar to Ayvalık's but with the additional quality that islands always have — a defined perimeter, an understanding that the water is close, a pace that is adjusted accordingly.

The ruined Church of Taxiarchis stands at the island's centre: a large 19th-century Orthodox church left open to the sky since the population exchange, its frescoes fading, fig trees growing through the floor. It is the most direct physical encounter with the history of this coast that the region offers, and it is completely free and completely unmanaged.

Photographer's Note

The Church of Taxiarchis in late afternoon: the light comes through the open roof and the missing walls and catches the remaining fresco fragments. The fig tree growing through the nave is the composition. Arrive when the sun is 30 degrees or below.

Aegean Table

What to Eat in Balıkesir

Olive oil, seafood and the island breakfast

The Ayvalık region produces olive oil that is sold across Turkey under various labels and that is, in its local unfiltered form, a greenish, intensely grassy oil of a quality that is worth purchasing in quantity. The morning breakfast on Cunda Island — local cheese, olive oil, olives from the nearest grove, bread from the bakery two streets away, eggs from the farm above the town — is a version of Turkish breakfast where the distance from field to table is under five kilometres. That proximity is tasted.

Seafood in Ayvalık follows the Aegean logic: simple preparation, fresh fish, a relationship with the water that is functional rather than decorative. The best fish restaurants are not the ones with harbour-view terraces. They are the small places one street back, where the menu is written on a chalkboard and changes daily with whatever came off the boats that morning.

05
Planning

When to Visit Balıkesir

Spring for the mountain, early summer for the coast

April and May in Kazdağları: the park is at its greenest, the trails are clear, and the tourist infrastructure has not yet geared up for summer. The mountain in spring has a particular quality of freshness that July, when it is at peak capacity, does not. For Ayvalık and Cunda, May and September are the ideal months. The Aegean is swimmable, the harbour is not yet full, and the light in both months is the particular low-angle, golden-hour-extended version that makes this coast cinematically difficult to leave.

July and August are functional. The towns handle the summer volume. But the quieter version — the morning market, the empty island streets before nine, the sunset from the harbour without a crowd — belongs to the shoulder months.
