Coast and Antiquity

Kemer: What the Resorts Are Standing In Front Of

Phaselis has three harbours and a pine forest that walks into the sea. Olympos has a river running through a ruined Roman city. Yanartaş has been burning since before the Iliad was written. Kemer is the address all of this shares.

By Halil BekarApril 20268 min read

The standard Kemer visit is a wristband and an infinity pool. That version of the coast is legitimate and functions every summer without complaint. It is also the version that keeps the better things at a comfortable distance.

The Taurus mountains descend directly into the Mediterranean at Kemer, which means the landscape behind the resort strip is vertical, forested and full of things that were here long before the first hotel was built. Phaselis alone is worth a day. Olympos is worth another. Yanartaş, once you understand what you are looking at, is worth a night.

01
Ancient Harbour

Phaselis

The city that Alexander actually liked

Phaselis was founded in the 7th century BC and occupies a peninsula where the Taurus mountains touch the sea in a way that creates three separate bays within a single archaeological site. Alexander the Great wintered here in 333 BC and found the city agreeable enough to make it a formal ally, which is the kind of historical endorsement that does not expire.

The ruins are distributed across pine forest between the bays: the agora, the main street colonnade, the bath complex, and the small theatre all set among trees whose roots have been working through the stones for centuries. The effect is of a city that was not excavated but simply allowed to remain in its landscape. In April, before the summer heat makes the forest airless, walking the paths between the ruins with the sea visible through the pines on both sides is an experience that Kemer's resort infrastructure cannot approach.

Alexander wintered at Phaselis and found it agreeable. The Taurus mountains and the pine forest have not changed their position. The ruins have added their own texture.

Photographer's Note

Phaselis in the early morning before 9am: the ruins are empty, the pine shadows are long and the north harbour has direct morning light. The combination of standing Roman columns, pine trees and clear Mediterranean water is the image that explains why Phaselis is not interchangeable with any other site on this coast.

02
River Ruins

Olympos

Where a Roman city meets its own river

Olympos is the archaeological site where a river runs directly through the ruins and exits onto the beach. The combination is not metaphorical: the actual stone walls of the ancient city rise from both sides of an actual river that you walk along, wade across in some sections, and eventually follow to a beach where the valley opens to the sea. The site has not been cleared into an open-air museum format. It has been allowed to exist in the condition it reached after centuries of overgrowth, which means the ruins feel found rather than displayed.

The treehouse accommodation culture that grew up around Olympos in the 1990s still exists in some form. For visitors interested in the archaeological site itself, arriving early in the morning before the day trippers from Antalya reach the valley is the practical imperative.

Traveler's Note

The Olympos beach at the end of the valley is not accessible by road. You reach it through the ruins. That quality of distance from the parking infrastructure keeps it in a different register from the resort beaches 15 kilometres away.

03
Eternal Flame

Yanartaş

The fire that predates the Iliad

Yanartaş is a hillside above the Olympos valley where natural gas seeps through the limestone and has been burning continuously for at least 2,500 years. Ancient sources describe the flames. Bellerophon's battle with the Chimaera is identified with this location. The fire is real, constant, and produces no heat visible from a distance because the individual flames are relatively small. At night they are visible from several kilometres out to sea, which is how sailors knew they were approaching this coast in the ancient world.

The site is a thirty-minute walk from the Olympos road on a path that winds through forest. Visit at night. During the day the flames are visible but the surrounding forest normalises them. At night, the scattered fire on a dark hillside with no other artificial light visible is genuinely strange in a way that daylight removes.

Photographer's Note

Yanartaş at night, ideally in a new moon period: long exposure, 15-25 seconds, with the flame clusters as the primary subject. The surrounding dark forest frames the fire points. If you can get above the site on the hillside, shoot down toward the sea so the fires appear against the dark water in the distance.

04
Active Adventure

Göynük Canyon

The canyon that requires water entry

Göynük Canyon is accessed by wading into the river at the canyon entrance and following the gorge upstream. The walls rise 20 metres on both sides, the water is cold in any season and waist-deep in some sections, and the route takes three to four hours for the complete circuit. It is not a subtle experience. The canyon infrastructure includes ziplines and platforms for the adventure tourism segment, but the basic act of walking through the gorge in the water is available to anyone willing to get wet.

Traveler's Note

Göynük is operationally simple: rent water shoes at the entrance if you did not bring your own, follow the river upstream, return the same way. The organised tours are not necessary. The canyon is not complex enough to require a guide.

05
Planning

When to Visit Kemer

The case against July and August

April and May are the optimal months. Phaselis is accessible without summer heat, Olympos is not yet crowded with day trippers, the pine forests are at their most fragrant, and Yanartaş can be visited without competing with the high-season Antalya package tours. The sea is swimmable from late April.

October is the second window. The summer crowd has returned to northern Europe, the archaeological sites are quiet, and the sea holds enough warmth for swimming well into the month. July and August are functional but the experience of Phaselis and Olympos in summer is a different and lesser thing than either site visited in its natural quietude.
