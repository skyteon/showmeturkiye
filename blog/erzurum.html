Erzurum: East Anatolia's Silent and Proud Fortress

At 1,900 metres above sea level, Erzurum is Turkey's coldest large city and one of its most architecturally serious ones. The Selçuk builders who worked here were not making concessions to comfort.

By Halil BekarApril 20268 min read

Some cities give themselves up immediately. Erzurum does not. The first impression is cold, horizontal and unsentimental. It takes a full day for the city to start making its case.

Erzurum sits at nearly 1,900 metres in the Eastern Anatolian plateau, where the winters are serious and the summers are short enough to feel borrowed. This climate has shaped the city's architecture, its food and, in some register that is hard to articulate, its character. Erzurum does not perform for visitors. It continues regardless of whether you are there to observe it.

01
Selçuk Architecture

Çifte Minareli Medrese

The most underrated monument in eastern Turkey

The Çifte Minareli Medrese was built in the late 13th century — its foundation inscription has not survived, and the exact date remains debated among historians, with most placing construction between 1285 and 1290 during the Ilkhanid period and is one of the most significant examples of Anatolian Selçuk architecture in existence. The twin minarets give the building its name and its silhouette, but the real quality is in the carved stone portal: a level of craft detail that would attract serious attention in a European capital's national museum. In Erzurum it stands on a public square and people walk past it daily on their way to work.

The interior courtyard is open to visitors. The cells that once housed students surround the central space with a geometry that is rational and severe. The building is not decorated in the way that Ottoman mosques are decorated. It is structured, resolved and serious. The aesthetic is entirely consistent with the city that contains it.

The Çifte Minareli Medrese's stone carving would fill a museum in Istanbul. In Erzurum it stands on a public square and the pigeons are indifferent.

Photographer's Note

The Çifte Minareli Medrese faces south. Photograph it in the morning from the north side: the minarets are backlit and the carved portal is in shadow, which reveals its depth better than direct sun. Overcast days are ideal for the stone texture.

The Mountain Behind

Palandöken

The ski mountain that sits inside the city's view

Palandöken rises directly behind Erzurum city to 2,935 metres and is visible from nearly every street in the city. In winter, when the slopes are open, the sight of a functioning ski mountain attached to a city of 500,000 people is unusual enough to make you look twice. The ski infrastructure is Turkey's most developed after Uludağ, with consistent snowfall from November through April.

In summer the mountain converts to hiking and ATV terrain. The upper slopes are at an altitude where the eastern steppe gives way to something cooler and more exposed, and the views back down to the Erzurum plain extend for an improbable distance in every direction.

Traveler's Note

The Üç Kümbetler, three 12th-century Selçuk tombs in a public park near the city centre, are almost never included in Erzurum itineraries. They are among the finest funerary monuments of their period and they are completely unguarded and free to enter.

What to Eat in Erzurum

Cağ kebabı and the horizontal spit

Cağ kebabı is the dish that Erzurum owns completely. The meat, lamb or a lamb-beef mix, is placed on a horizontal spit and cooked over an open flame while rotating. Thin slices are cut directly onto bread as they cook. The result is crisp on the exterior, soft inside, and carries a smokiness that vertical doner cannot replicate. The best cağ kebabı is served with nothing more than raw onion and flatbread. The restraint is the point.

The city's bazaar area near the Rüstem Paşa Caravanserai contains craftsmen working with oltu taşı, a black lignite stone found only in the Erzurum-Oltu region. Jewellery, prayer beads and decorative objects carved from oltu are a genuine Erzurum artefact, not a generic souvenir.

Traveler's Note

Erzurum's breakfast culture is serious. The cheese produced in the highland farms above the city is distinct from western Turkish varieties: harder, saltier, with a sharper edge. Combined with local honey and butter from the same farms, it makes an argument for staying an extra morning.

Planning

When to Visit Erzurum

Two seasons with distinct arguments

December through March is the ski season and the city is at its most purposeful. Everything functions, the restaurants are full, and the mountain is the reason people are there. The cold is real but not debilitating if you are dressed for it.

May through September is the cultural visit season. The monuments are accessible without snow cover, the highland roads are open, and the Palandöken routes convert to summer walking. June is the best single month: the plateau has not yet dried out, the air is clear and cool, and the city's pace is measured.
