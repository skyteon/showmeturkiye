Ordu: The Black Sea Coast That Doesn't Try to Explain Itself

A city on a hazelnut coast, a plateau with river meanders that look like a cartographic argument, and a cable car that delivers you above the cloud line before the city has finished its morning tea.

By Show Me Türkiye TeamApril 20268 min read
Scroll

Ordu sits on the Black Sea coast between Giresun and Samsun, which means most people in a car reach it, look at the sea briefly, and continue. This is a calibration error.

The city has a cable car to a hilltop tea garden that rises above the cloud cover on most mornings. It has a hazelnut production that makes it the global centre of a crop that exists in almost every chocolate bar on earth. And it has the Perşembe plateau above it, where rivers loop in meanders so tight they form almost complete circles in a landscape that at certain angles looks designed rather than geological. The city does not advertise these things aggressively. They exist regardless.

01
City High Point

Boztepe

The view that the cable car makes worth taking

Boztepe is the hill directly above Ordu city centre, accessible by road or by a cable car that runs from the waterfront. At 515 metres, it sits above the Black Sea fog layer on most mornings — which means that what you ascend into from the cloud-grey city below is a hilltop in direct sun, the sea invisible beneath white cloud on one side, the forested interior hills visible on the other. The tea gardens on the summit plateau operate at a pace calibrated to people who are not trying to accomplish anything.

In the evening, when the fog has lifted and the light comes from the west at a low angle, the view from Boztepe contains the city below, the harbour, the Black Sea horizon, and on very clear days the curve of the coast continuing eastward toward Giresun. This is one of the few elevated urban viewpoints in the Black Sea region where the combination of city, sea and highland interior is fully visible simultaneously.

The cable car rises through cloud. At the top, direct sun and a tea garden and a view over a sea you cannot see from the city below. The Black Sea plays its geography games with its own logic.

Photographer's Note

Boztepe from the city at dusk: the hill silhouetted against the western sky, the cable car cabins visible as moving points against the darkening forest. Shoot from the waterfront promenade looking northwest. The harbour lights start to come on around the same time the sky turns.

02
Highland River Loops

Perşembe Plateau

The meanders that look impossible from above

The Perşembe plateau sits above 1,000 metres and contains a river system that has developed meanders — wide, looping bends — of the kind that normally form in flat, low-gradient landscapes. On a highland plateau, surrounded by hills, the meanders look like the river has been debating which direction to travel and keeps changing its mind within a small radius. From the viewpoints above the valley, each loop is almost a complete circle. Sheep graze inside the inner curves. The effect is of a landscape that a draughtsman designed rather than water erosion produced.

The plateau is accessible from Perşembe district, approximately 60 kilometres from Ordu city. The roads are paved but narrow and follow the contour of the terrain rather than cutting through it. This is not a drive for people who need to arrive quickly. It is a drive for people who have decided that the journey is already the destination.

Traveler's Note

The Perşembe plateau in early June: the meadow grasses are tall, the livestock have recently moved up from the winter pastures, and the morning mist sits in the lowest parts of the meander loops before the sun burns it off. That forty-minute window is the version of the plateau that photographs can barely contain.

03
Mountain Lake

Ulugöl Nature Park

80 kilometres and a thousand metres up from the coast

Uluğöl Lake is approximately 80 kilometres from Ordu city centre and sits at around 1,400 metres, surrounded by beech and spruce forest. The lake is fed by springs and has the clarity characteristic of high-altitude forest water. The national park infrastructure around it is restrained: walking paths, a small campsite, basic facilities. The correct relationship with this place is an early start from Ordu, arrival before other visitors, and several hours of slow walking around the perimeter path.

The drive up from the coast is part of the experience. The Black Sea escarpment here is steep and the road gains altitude quickly, transitioning from coastal hazelnut orchards to dense beech forest within twenty kilometres. The forest changes character as the altitude rises, and by the time you reach the lake the coastal city seems improbable.

Photographer's Note

Uluğöl at dawn before 7am: the lake surface is undisturbed, the forest reflection is sharp, and the beech canopy overhead is the only moving thing. A 10-minute exposure of the still water produces a glass-like surface effect. Bring a tripod. The light lasts about 45 minutes before it changes.

04
Cape

Cape Jason

The headland that mythology and geography share

Cape Jason extends into the Black Sea at the western end of Ordu province and carries a name connected to the Argonautica — Jason and the Argonauts are said to have stopped here on the voyage east. Whether or not the connection is literal, the cape's topography earns its mythological association: cliffs on the seaward side, forested slopes on the landward approach, and a view at the headland where the Black Sea becomes visible in three directions simultaneously.

The cape is accessible by road and is not a managed tourist site. There is a lighthouse, a small number of facilities, and a path to the cliff edge. Sunset from the headland in autumn, when the Black Sea changes from blue to grey-green in the fading light, is the version of this place that rewards the journey.

Traveler's Note

Sunset at Cape Jason in September: the summer crowds have gone, the sea has begun its seasonal colour shift, and the light from the west catches the cliff faces at a low angle for about thirty minutes. Stay until the lighthouse comes on. That detail makes the image.

05
Planning

When to Visit Ordu

Two seasons and what each offers

May and June are ideal. The plateau meanders are at their most dramatic in terms of vegetation, the hazelnut orchards are in leaf, the mountain lakes are clear and cold, and Boztepe's cloud-sea inversion happens most reliably in the late spring months. The Black Sea coast weather is unpredictable but the landscape rewards the unpredictability.

August brings the hazelnut harvest, which is the economic pulse of the entire province. The harvest involves essentially the whole rural population in a coordinated seasonal effort that is visible from any road passing through the groves. September and October are the quieter, cooler version of summer: the crowds have left, the coast retains enough warmth for swimming, and the beech forest above the city begins turning gold in October in a way that the hazelnut groves below it, which stay green, frame rather than participate in.
