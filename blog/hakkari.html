Hakkari: The Province That Refuses to Be Simplified

Where Turkey shares borders with Iraq and Iran, the mountains go vertical and the landscape stops making concessions to legibility. Hakkari earns its reputation as the country's most honest terrain.

By Halil BekarApril 20269 min read
Scroll

Hakkari is the place on the Turkish map that most people enlarge and then feel uncertain about. The closer you zoom, the more complex it becomes.

It sits at the southeastern corner of the country where Turkey, Iraq and Iran share borders within a few kilometres. The terrain is not dramatic in the way that makes tourists feel comfortable. It is dramatic in the way that makes cartographers nervous. Valleys drop hundreds of metres between ridgelines that barely appear navigable. The province resists summary, which is the most accurate thing you can say about it.

01
High Peaks

Cilo and Sat Mountains

Turkey's third highest summit

The Cilo massif contains Reşko Zirvesi at 4,135 metres, the third highest point in Turkey. Unlike more accessible eastern mountains, Cilo is approached through the Hakkari valleys on roads that require serious commitment. The Zap valley descent, canyon walls closing in, the road following a river that has been cutting this gorge for geological time. The approach is part of the experience.

The Sat range runs parallel, slightly lower, equally uncompromising. Between them, highland meadows exist at altitude, seasonally populated by semi-nomadic shepherds who have been running these routes for generations. In June and July the upper plateau has a saturated green that entirely contradicts the rock-and-canyon landscape you drove through to reach it.

Hakkari's mountains don't perform for visitors. They continue regardless. You either adjust to that or you leave early.

Photographer's Note

The Zap valley road between Van and Hakkari passes through canyon sections where walls are 200 metres vertical on both sides. Pull over at widened sections and shoot upstream. Morning gives east-facing canyon light. Afternoon gives the west face.

02
Zap Tributary

Nehil Valley

Turquoise water that should not exist here

The Nehil valley is formed by a Zap tributary, and the water that flows through it is a turquoise that reads as wrong in context. You are surrounded by grey and brown canyon rock, and the river at the floor is a colour more associated with the Aegean. The explanation is geology: limestone filtering and glacial melt from the upper Cilo peaks. The experience is simply jarring in the best way.

The valley is accessible by road from Hakkari centre, roughly 25 kilometres. The last section is unpaved. The reward is a river that appears to have been borrowed from somewhere else and installed in this canyon without asking permission.

Traveler's Note

The Yüksekova plain, 60 kilometres from Hakkari city, sits at 1,800 metres surrounded by rocky ridges that create a landscape entirely unlike the valleys. It is accessed on a separate road and is frequently overlooked.

03
Logistics

How to Get to Hakkari

The practical version

The most efficient route is to fly to Van airport and drive south. Van to Hakkari is approximately three and a half hours, and the Zap canyon road makes the drive worth scheduling rather than minimising. There is no direct airport in Hakkari province.

For trekking in the Cilo and Sat ranges, use a local guide. The mountain terrain is not marked to western standard and the consequences of navigation errors at altitude are serious. Several trekking operations based in Hakkari offer guided access to the high routes with appropriate equipment and local knowledge.

04
Planning

When to Visit Hakkari

The narrow window

June through September is the accessible window. Mountain passes open in late May and close in October, sometimes earlier. June gives the alpine meadows at their most saturated, wildflowers on the upper slopes, snowfields still visible on the Cilo peaks. August is the high season for serious trekking.

Outside that window the high terrain is inaccessible without specialist winter equipment. The city and lower valleys remain accessible year-round, but the defining mountain landscape is strictly seasonal.
