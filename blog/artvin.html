Artvin: Where the Çoruh Canyon Refuses to Let You Pass

A province where the river drops through gorges that require engineers to admire them, the highlands erupt green above 2,000 metres, and the Georgian monasteries sit in valleys that no signpost adequately prepares you for.

By Show Me Türkiye TeamApril 20269 min read
Scroll

Artvin is a province where the mountains do not stand apart from the valleys. They fall into them, directly, without transition.

The Çoruh River runs through a canyon that drops 800 metres from the surrounding plateau. The road that follows it is one of the most dramatic stretches of driving in the country, and most people who take it are going somewhere else. The ones who stop, who get out and stand at the edge and understand what the river has cut through that rock over geological time, tend to stay longer than they planned.

01
The River That Defines the Province

Çoruh Valley and Rafting

260 kilometres of white water and canyon

The Çoruh River runs approximately 260 kilometres from Bayburt through Yusufeli and Artvin to the Georgian border, dropping through a canyon that makes it one of the most technically demanding rafting rivers in Europe. The difficulty levels run from Grade 2 to Grade 5, and the 4th World Rafting Championship was held here in 1993. Professional athletes come specifically for the river. Most visitors simply drive alongside it and look.

The Yusufeli stretch before the valley opens toward Artvin city is the most dramatic. The road is carved directly into the canyon wall in sections. The river below runs turquoise-green in summer from glacial sediment. The valley walls are so close that on overcast days the light is halfway between canyon and tunnel. This is not a drive to make quickly.

The Çoruh was used for the World Rafting Championship not despite being difficult but because of it. The river does not accommodate anything. You accommodate the river.

Photographer's Note

The Çoruh canyon is best photographed from the road pull-offs between Yusufeli and Artvin. Morning light enters the canyon from the east and catches the water directly. Afternoon turns the canyon walls amber and the water blue-green. Both are worth stopping for.

02
Crater Lake

Borçka Karagöl

The lake that has no reason to be that colour

Karagöl sits at approximately 1,300 metres inside the Borçka district forests, and the drive to reach it is part of the reason to go. The road climbs steeply from the Çoruh valley floor, the spruce and fir close in, and then without gradual preparation the lake appears: a deep emerald-green body of water occupying what appears to be a small glacial depression, entirely surrounded by forest with no visual exits. The colour is generated by suspended minerals and the specific depth of the water. It does not look enhanced. It looks implausible.

The facilities are basic but adequate — a boat hire point, a shoreside walking path, a small cafe. The correct experience is to arrive early in the morning before the day visitors from Artvin city, walk the perimeter path slowly, and sit at the far end of the lake where the reflection of the fir trees on the water is the entirety of the view.

Traveler's Note

Karagöl in autumn, October specifically, is the other version of this lake. The fir trees on the southern shore remain green, the deciduous trees on the north turn gold and orange, and the reflection contains both simultaneously. It is one of the most underestimated autumn colour scenes in the country.

03
Alpine Plateau

Kaçkar Highlands and Savşat

Above the tree line, into the grazing country

The Kaçkar range forms the eastern backbone of the Black Sea highlands, and the Artvin slopes of it are the least-visited section. The Savşat district on the northeastern side of the range produces landscapes of highland meadow, traditional stone farmhouses, and ridgelines that on clear mornings appear at the edge of the sky before the rest of the terrain fills in below them. The altitude plateau here is accessible by road from late May to October and on foot year-round for those with proper equipment.

The Hatila Valley National Park near Artvin city itself — accessible from the city centre in under an hour — is a forested gorge with a river running through it, waterfalls at intervals, and a bird population that draws specialist observers from across Europe during the spring and autumn migration corridors. The Artvin region sits on one of the primary Black Sea bird migration routes.

Photographer's Note

The Hatila Valley gorge in morning light: the forest canopy closes over the river in sections, filtering the light to individual shafts. The path follows the river closely enough that the sound of the water is constant. Shoot downstream, looking toward where the gorge opens, in the hour after sunrise.

04
Georgian Heritage

The Medieval Monasteries

10th-century stone churches in active valleys

The Artvin region formed part of the medieval Kingdom of Georgia and contains several monasteries and churches from that period that sit in active agricultural valleys without interpretive barriers, fences, or significant tourist infrastructure. The Dörtkilise monastery complex near Yusufeli, the Ishan church near the same district, and the Öşkvank monastery are among the most significant surviving examples of Georgian ecclesiastical architecture in Türkiye. They are constructed from the same local stone as the valley walls and have been absorbing light for over a thousand years.

These sites require some research and navigation to locate correctly. The roads to them are often unmarked or marked only in Turkish on older signs. The experience of finding them without help — turning a corner on a mountain road and encountering a 10th-century church standing intact in a walnut orchard — is part of what makes the Artvin region unlike anywhere else in the country.

Traveler's Note

The Ishan Church near Yüsufeli is one of the finest examples of medieval Georgian architecture in Türkiye. It requires asking locally for directions. It is not on most maps. It is also completely free and completely unspoiled. That combination is rare enough to justify the navigation effort.

05
Planning

When to Visit Artvin

The window and what each season offers

May through October is the full-access window. The highland roads open in late May and close again with the first heavy snow in October or November. June brings the highland meadows to their most saturated state; July and August are the peak months for the Çoruh rafting season. September is arguably the best single month: the highland routes are open, the river is still runnable, and the crowds have thinned.

Autumn colour in October is exceptional — Artvin province has the densest concentration of deciduous forest at altitude in the eastern Black Sea region, and when it turns in mid-October the result is one of the most visually dramatic landscapes the country produces. October camping in the highlands requires cold-weather equipment. It is worth it.
