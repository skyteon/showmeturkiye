Why Visit

Antalya

Antalya is a city that takes people by surprise. Most visitors arrive expecting a beach resort and find instead a layered, complex place with Roman ruins sitting alongside Ottoman houses, a working old port full of wooden gullets, and a market culture that has not changed much in centuries. The mountains pressing in from the north are part of the deal. On clear days the snow-capped Taurus range hangs directly above the turquoise sea, and that combination is hard to find anywhere else.

Kalçi, the old town, is the real heart of the city. Walk through Hadrian's Gate and the modern city disappears. Narrow streets, restored mansions converted into boutique hotels, a harbour where Roman ships once anchored. There are Hellenistic walls still standing, Byzantine churches converted into mosques, and a clock tower from the 19th century. All of this is within walking distance of each other.

Outside the city, the ancient sites are extraordinary. Aspendos has one of the best-preserved Roman theatres in the world. Perge is vast and mostly empty in the early morning. Termessos sits at over 1,000 metres in the Taurus and Alexander the Great himself decided it was not worth trying to take. Three to four days is the minimum to do any of this properly.

Places to Visit

01. Kaleiçi Old Town
Kaleiçi Old Town

Antalya's walled old quarter has been inhabited continuously since the Romans built it in the 2nd century BC. Walk in through Hadrian's Gate and you step into a network of cobbled streets lined with restored Ottoman mansions, small mosques and boutique hotels. The old harbour at the southern end is where wooden gullets have moored for two thousand years. Go on a weekday morning before the tour groups arrive.

02. Aspendos Theatre
Aspendos Theatre

Built around 155 AD by the architect Zenon, this Roman theatre is widely considered one of the best-preserved in the world. The stage building is almost fully intact. The acoustics are extraordinary. A person speaking from the stage at normal volume can be heard clearly in the back row. It seats around 7,000 people and still hosts the annual Aspendos Opera and Ballet Festival in summer. It is 47 km east of Antalya city centre. Go before 10am to have the space mostly to yourself.

03. Perge Ancient City
Perge Ancient City

One of the great cities of ancient Pamphylia, Perge lies 17 km east of Antalya. St Paul preached his first sermon here in 46 AD. The ruins include a monumental gate, a colonnaded main street with a central water channel that still impresses, a massive stadium that could hold 15,000 spectators, and extensive baths. The site is large and takes two to three hours to walk properly. It gets very hot by midday so arrive early.

04. Termessos
Termessos

An ancient Pisidian city at over 1,000 metres in the Taurus Mountains, 30 km northwest of Antalya. Alexander the Great surrounded it in 333 BC, compared it to an eagle's nest and decided the assault was not worth the cost. The ruins include a theatre carved into the mountainside with views down to the sea, a gymnasium, tombs cut into the rock, and the agora. The site is inside a national park and is genuinely wild. Wear proper shoes and bring water.

05. Düden Waterfalls
Düden Waterfalls

The Düden River comes down from the Taurus Mountains and splits into two sets of falls. The Upper Düden is in a shaded park about 12 km northeast of the city centre. A path leads behind the main cascade into a cave, which is unusual and worth seeing. The Lower Düden, in the Lara district, drops 40 metres straight into the Mediterranean from a cliff edge and is best seen from a boat cruise departing Kalçi harbour.

06. Konyaaltı Beach
Konyaaltı Beach

A 7 km pebble and sand beach stretching west from the city with the Taurus Mountains as a backdrop. It has Blue Flag status and is well organised with beach clubs, cafes and water sports facilities. The promenade running alongside it is one of the better urban seafront walks in southern Turkiye. Best in the early morning and late afternoon to avoid the peak heat of summer.

Food and Cuisine

Antalya has a food culture built around what the land and sea produce here. The region has its own vegetables, its own olive oil, its own fish, and a set of dishes that belong specifically to this part of the Mediterranean. Piyaz, the local white bean salad, is so central to the cuisine that locals measure a restaurant by how well they make it. The market behind the old bazaar is worth a morning on its own.

The Signature
Piyaz

Antalya piyaz is not the piyaz you find elsewhere. It is made with boiled white beans, tahini, hard-boiled eggs, parsley, olives and a sharp vinegar-lemon dressing. Every lokanta has its own version but the Antalya style is creamier and more substantial than other regional variations. It is typically eaten as a side dish with kofte or grilled meat.

The Classic
Antalya Kofte

Hand-formed beef or lamb meatballs grilled over charcoal, served with piyaz and sliced tomatoes. Simple and very good. The combination of kofte and piyaz is so standard here that most restaurants serve them as a fixed pairing. Do not skip the bread used to soak up the piyaz dressing.

The Sea
Grilled Sea Bass

The Mediterranean off Antalya produces good sea bass, sea bream and red mullet. The fish restaurants along the harbour and on the Konyaalt waterfront grill them simply with olive oil and herbs. The best ones have a short menu and change it depending on what came in that morning. Ask what is fresh before ordering.

Market Find
Citrus and Pomegranate

Antalya province produces enormous quantities of citrus fruit. The orange season runs from November to March. Pomegranates are in season in autumn. Fresh juice stands operate at the market and throughout the old town all year. Pomegranate molasses is used in many local sauces and salad dressings and is sold in bulk at the bazaar.

Morning Staple
Antalya Breakfast

A proper Antalya breakfast involves local honey from the Taurus Mountains, fresh olives from the region, regional white cheese, tomatoes and cucumbers from surrounding farms, and bread from the neighbourhood bakery. It is served slowly, with tea refilled often, and is rarely eaten before 9am. Many locals consider Sunday breakfast a near-sacred ritual.

Sweet Finish
Kabak Tatlısı

Pumpkin dessert cooked in sugar syrup until soft and translucent, served with a spoonful of tahini and crushed walnuts on top. It looks simple and is actually remarkable. The pumpkins grown in the Antalya region have a particular sweetness that makes the local version better than most. Available in season from late summer through winter.

On the Ground

Activities and Experiences

01
Walk Kaleiçi at Dawn

The old town before 8am is a different place. The tour groups have not arrived, the light is soft, the cats are still everywhere. Walk from Hadrian's Gate down to the harbour. Stop at a small tea house that is already open and sit for twenty minutes. The narrow streets take on a different quality at that hour. It is the best version of Kalçi.

02
Day Trip to Aspendos and Perge

Both ancient sites are east of the city and can be combined in one day with a car. Leave by 8am, Perge first as it is closer. Two hours there, then drive 35 km further to Aspendos. Spend the rest of the morning at the theatre. The drive back passes through productive agricultural land with citrus groves and greenhouses. The total round trip is around 100 km.

03
Boat Tour from the Old Harbour

Wooden gullets depart from Kalçi harbour daily, usually around 10am. A standard half-day tour takes you along the cliffs west of the city, past the Lower Düden where the waterfall drops into the sea, and out to several small coves for swimming stops. The view of the city from the water, with the Taurus mountains behind it, is something that photographs do not quite capture.

Further Afield

Day Trips

75 km East, about 1 hour 15 min
Side and Manavgat

Side is a small peninsula with Roman ruins sitting alongside the beach. The Temple of Apollo at the water's edge is one of the more dramatic ancient sites in the country. Manavgat, a few kilometres inland, has a large waterfall that is popular with locals. The market in Manavgat on Sundays is a proper agricultural and artisan market, nothing tourist-oriented.

30 km Northwest, about 45 min
Termessos National Park

Combining the ancient city of Termessos with the national park surrounding it makes for a full day out of the city. The hike to the main ruins takes about an hour from the car park and the terrain is steep. The vegetation changes as you climb through pine and cedar forest. The theatre, sitting on a ledge with a view all the way to the sea, is the reward for the effort.
