Why Visit

Muğla

Muğla Province holds the highest concentration of remarkable coastline in Turkey. The list of what is here is genuinely impressive: the Blue Lagoon at Ölüdeniz, which photographs suggest and reality confirms; Bodrum's medieval castle built by the Knights of St John from the stones of the Mausoleum, one of the Seven Wonders; the Dalyan delta where loggerhead turtles nest on Iztuzu Beach; the Gulf of Gökova for sailing; Marmaris with its ancient citadel above a modern harbour; and the Lycian Way that runs through the province connecting ruins and coastline on foot.

Each of the major destinations here Bodrum, Ölüdeniz, Marmaris, Dalyan functions independently and has its own character. Bodrum is the most culturally dense, with archaeology, nightlife, a serious food scene and the best museum of underwater antiquities in the world. Ölüdeniz is for the water and the air. Dalyan is for the river, the ruins, the mud baths and the turtles. Marmaris is the sailing hub.

A proper tour of Muğla requires at least a week. The roads between the destinations are good and renting a car gives the most freedom to find the quieter bays in between.

Places to Visit

01. Ölüdeniz Blue Lagoon
Ölüdeniz

The most photographed beach in Turkey and for good reason: a protected lagoon of still turquoise water separated from the open sea by a sandbar, with pine-covered mountains rising directly from the shore. The lagoon is a national nature reserve and the water is exceptionally clear. The paragliding from Babadağ (1,969 m) above delivers one of the most dramatic flight routes in the world. 14 km south of Fethiye. Busiest July to August.

02. Bodrum Castle
Bodrum Castle

Built from 1402 by the Knights of St John using stones from the Mausoleum of Halicarnassus (one of the Seven Wonders). Four towers named after the European nations that funded them surround a harbour that has been active since antiquity. The castle now houses the Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology, which holds the most important collection of ancient shipwreck cargo in the world. Open Tuesday to Sunday. Allow three hours for the full museum circuit.

03. Dalyan and Iztuzu Beach
Dalyan

The Dalyan delta is a protected waterway running through reed beds between the ruins of Kaunos and the long barrier beach of Iztuzu, a primary nesting site for loggerhead sea turtles (Caretta caretta). Boat trips from Dalyan town cover the rock-cut Lycian tombs at Kaunos, the sulphur mud baths, the thermal springs at Sultaniye and Iztuzu Beach in a single day. Nesting season June to September means part of the beach is roped off at night.

04. Marmaris Castle and Old Town
Marmaris

The 16th-century Ottoman fortress sits above the old bazaar quarter with views over the pine-covered peninsula and the harbour below. Suleiman the Magnificent used Marmaris as his staging post before the siege of Rhodes in 1522. The castle now functions as a small museum and the surrounding old bazaar lanes are the most atmospheric part of the modern resort. The marina below is the largest Blue Cruise departure point in Turkey.

05. Kayaköy Ghost Village
Kayaköy

A deserted Greek village of 3,500 stone houses, churches and public buildings abandoned during the 1923 population exchange between Greece and Turkey. The buildings stand on a hillside exactly as they were left, slowly crumbling. Walking the lanes with no residents and no sound beyond the wind is an unusual experience. 8 km north of Ölüdeniz. UNESCO tentative heritage status. Open year round. No entrance fee for the village itself; small charge for the main church.

06. Butterfly Valley
Kelebekler Vadisi

A steep canyon accessible only by boat (from Ölüdeniz) or on foot down a difficult trail, with a waterfall, a camping area and no roads in. Named for the Jersey Tiger moth that breeds here in summer. The boat from Ölüdeniz runs in summer season, takes 20 minutes, and includes return trips at set times. An overnight stay in the camping area is the reason many people make the effort. No permanent accommodation beyond basic camping.

Local Cuisine

What to Eat

Muğla's food is Aegean Mediterranean: fresh seafood, olive oil, wild herbs, grilled fish and the light, vegetable-heavy cooking that defines the southwestern coast. Bodrum has a more sophisticated restaurant scene than any other district in the province, with serious fish restaurants along the waterfront and the marina area. The rest of the province eats better than it gets credit for.

Aegean Seafood
Levrek and Çipura

Sea bass and sea bream, farmed in the bays of the Aegean and Mediterranean, are the two fish you will eat most in Muğla. The best versions are grilled over charcoal with olive oil and lemon. Order by weight, ask for the daily catch rather than the menu price. The fish restaurants along the Bodrum marina stay open until late and sell at market rate with no tourist markup if you choose the right place.

Meze Culture
Aegean Meze

The Aegean meze tradition is lighter than the Istanbul version and heavier on vegetables and olive oil. Specific to the region: fava bean puree with dill, wild greens cooked with lemon, grilled octopus, stuffed courgette flowers in summer, sea urchin roe in winter months. Order four or five cold mezes and share across the table before the main fish. This is how a proper meal works here.

Bodrum Specialty
Bodrum Mandalinası

The tangerine orchards of Bodrum Peninsula produce a small, intensely flavoured fruit harvested from November to February. Bodrum tangerine is a protected geographical indication and sold fresh at local markets. It also appears as juice, jam and liqueur. The flavour is significantly more concentrated than standard tangerines. In season, buy a kilo from a roadside stand and eat them immediately.

Fish Market
Balık Pazarı

Bodrum's covered fish market operates mornings from Tuesday to Sunday. The catch includes local species: scorpionfish, sea bream, red mullet, squid, octopus and sea bass depending on season and conditions. Several restaurants adjacent to the market will cook what you buy for a preparation fee. This is the most honest and economical way to eat well in Bodrum. Go before 09:00 for the widest selection.

Fethiye Region
Pişi and Kahvaltı

The Fethiye and Ölüdeniz area has a strong village breakfast tradition. Pişi is fried dough eaten with honey, clotted cream and cheese. Local farm breakfasts in the villages above Ölüdeniz serve organic eggs, tomatoes, olives, hand-churned butter and fresh bread. Several small farms in the hills between Fethiye and Kayaköy open for breakfast in spring and summer. Worth seeking out over hotel buffets.

Sweet
Muğla Şambali

A semolina cake soaked in syrup, cut in diamond shapes and topped with crushed pistachio. Muğla's version uses local thyme honey in the syrup rather than plain sugar, which gives it a more complex, slightly herbal sweetness. Found at patisseries in Bodrum, Marmaris and the Muğla city centre. Best purchased as a gift in a boxed form from a local producer rather than a tourist shop version.

Where to Eat

Recommended Restaurants

Bodrum has the highest concentration of quality restaurants in the province. Most good options are on or near the waterfront.

Korfez Bodrum
★★★★★ 4.7 · 1,100+ reviews

A fish restaurant on the Bodrum waterfront known for quality produce and a straightforward approach. The daily catch is listed on the board at the entrance. Grilled whole fish with Aegean meze is the standard order. Tables on the terrace with views over the castle at night. Reserve ahead in July and August.

Grilled Fish, Aegean Meze, Local Wine
Meğri Lokantası
★★★★★ 4.6 · 900+ reviews

A traditional lokanta in Fethiye town, away from the tourist strip, where local workers eat lunch. The daily menu changes based on what came in that morning. Clay pot lamb, stuffed vegetables and fried red mullet are the reliable orders. Cash only, fills up by 12:30 and often runs out by 14:00.

Daily Stews, Fried Fish, Local Dishes
Deniz Restaurant Dalyan
★★★★★ 4.6 · 700+ reviews

A waterside restaurant on the Dalyan channel where boat tours pass. Good for lunch after a morning river trip: fresh river trout, grilled sea bass and cold meze on a terrace with views of the Lycian rock tombs carved into the cliff face directly opposite. The setting adds significantly to the meal.

River Trout, Sea Bass, Meze
Antique Theatre Bodrum
★★★★☆ 4.5 · 600+ reviews

A restaurant above Bodrum with terrace views over the castle and the bay. Good for dinner rather than lunch when the light softens and the castle is lit. The food is reliably good without being exceptional. Worth visiting once for the setting. Booking essential for terrace tables in high season.

Experiences

Things to Do

01
Paragliding from Babadağ

The tandem paragliding flight from Babadağ (1,969 m) above Ölüdeniz to the beach below takes 25 to 45 minutes depending on wind conditions. The approach over the lagoon from altitude is one of the most dramatic views of any coastline in Turkey. Multiple licensed operators run flights daily from April to November. Book through your hotel or directly at the base camp. The flight ends on Ölüdeniz beach. Price includes transport up the mountain.

02
Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology

Inside Bodrum Castle, this is the most important collection of ancient maritime archaeology in the world. Highlights include the Bronze Age Uluburun shipwreck cargo (14th century BC), with gold, copper, tin and glass from across the ancient Mediterranean; the glass wreck hall; and the Carian princess sarcophagus. The excavation was a joint project with Texas A&M University's Institute of Nautical Archaeology. Allow three hours minimum. Open Tuesday to Sunday.

03
Dalyan Boat Day

The standard Dalyan day tour runs on flat-bottomed wooden boats: past the Lycian rock tombs at Kaunos, to the thermal mud baths at Sultaniye, to the thermal springs, and finally to Iztuzu Beach for swimming. The route takes 4 to 5 hours and costs around 400 TL per person including lunch. Most operators depart from the main dock at 09:30. In June to September, the beach at Iztuzu is patrolled for turtle nests and sections may be closed at night.

Between Districts

Connecting the Coast

Lycian Way on Foot
The Lycian Way

A 540 km marked long-distance trail from Ölüdeniz to Antalya along the ancient Lycian coast. The most popular section runs from Ölüdeniz through Faralya, Butterfly Valley and Kabak, covering about 4 days of walking. The views from the clifftop path above the Mediterranean are consistently extraordinary. Stay in simple pensions along the route. April, May and October are the best months for temperature and wildflower cover.

Blue Cruise from Bodrum or Marmaris
Güllet Sailing

The traditional gulet wooden sailing boat routes between Bodrum and Marmaris pass through the Gulf of Gökova, stopping at remote bays accessible only by sea. A standard cruise takes 7 days and includes meals, cabin accommodation and a skipper. Routes run May to October. Charter prices vary by boat size and season. The quiet bays of the Datça Peninsula and the Hisarönü Gulf cannot be reached any other way.
