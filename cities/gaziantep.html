Why Visit

Gaziantep

Gaziantep has two claims that are hard to argue with. The first: it is the food city of Turkey. Not one of them, not arguably, but simply the one. The baklava alone could justify the trip. Made with pistachios grown 60 km away in Islahiye and butter from local sheep's milk, Antep baklava is a different thing entirely from what you find in Istanbul pastry shops. The second claim: the Zeugma Mosaic Museum is the largest mosaic museum in the world, and the mosaics inside are among the most extraordinary Roman-era artworks you will find anywhere.

The city itself has depth. The castle at the centre has stood since the Hittite era, the copper bazaar has been hammering out vessels for 400 years, and the old covered market called the Zincirli Bedesten still operates the way it always has. People from Antep are known across Turkey for their intensity around food. Ask about the correct way to make beyran soup and you will have a conversation that lasts an hour.

Two days is the minimum to eat and see properly. Three is better. The city rewards slowness.

Places to Visit

01. Zeugma Mosaic Museum
Zeugma Mosaic Museum

The largest mosaic museum in the world, with 2,248 square metres of Roman and late antique floor mosaics rescued from the ancient city of Zeugma before it was flooded by the Birecik Dam in 2000. The Gypsy Girl mosaic and the bronze Mars statue are the most famous pieces. Buy the audio guide. Allow at least two and a half hours. Open Tuesday to Sunday.

02. Gaziantep Castle
Gaziantep Castle

Built originally in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD on a Hittite observation mound, expanded by Byzantine Emperor Justinian I in the 6th century. Today it operates as the Gaziantep Defence and Heroism Panoramic Museum, documenting the city's resistance against French forces in 1920. The castle was damaged in the 2023 earthquake and restoration is ongoing. The views from the top over the old city remain excellent.

03. Bakırcılar Çarşısı
Bakırcılar Çarşısı

The Coppersmith Bazaar has been operating for nearly 400 years and still has working craftsmen at the benches. You can walk in, watch a piece being hammered and engraved, and buy directly from the maker. The sound of copper being worked is constant. It sits adjacent to the castle in the old bazaar area. Budget 90 minutes to walk through properly and explore the surrounding lanes.

04. Gaziantep Museum
Gaziantep Museum

The city's main archaeological museum, separate from Zeugma, holds finds from the broader region spanning the Paleolithic through Byzantine periods. A mammoth skeleton, Hittite carvings, Hellenistic sculptures and Ottoman artifacts are all here. Less crowded than Zeugma and entry is inexpensive. Worth two hours if the Zeugma has left you wanting more context.

05. Zincirli Bedesten
Zincirli Bedesten

The 15th-century Ottoman covered market in the heart of the old city, still selling textiles, spices, dried fruit and local produce. The domed stone ceiling keeps it cool in summer. This is not a tourist market. Most of the customers are locals doing their weekly shopping. The spice sellers along the central aisle carry Urfa biber, Maras pepper, dried herbs and things you will not find in ordinary shops.

06. Rumkale
Rumkale

A medieval Armenian fortress on a dramatic peninsula where the Fırat and Merzimen rivers meet, 85 km northeast of Gaziantep. The site is sacred to Christians who believe Saint John wrote and kept his gospel here. It is now partially submerged following the Birecik Dam but the upper fortifications remain accessible. Reached by boat from Halfeti or by road. Spectacular in late afternoon light.

Local Cuisine

What to Eat

Gaziantep holds a UNESCO Creative City of Gastronomy designation, and it earns it. The food here is precise. Antep cuisine is not simple or rustic. It is a cooking tradition that has been refined over centuries, using pistachio, lamb, bulgur, red pepper paste and Maras pepper in combinations that have clear rules. Locals are particular about what is and is not authentic. This specificity is part of what makes eating here so good.

Antep Signature
Antep Baklavası

Made with locally grown pistachios and clarified sheep's milk butter. The dough is rolled to near transparency, layered with crushed pistachio, baked and soaked in syrup. The version from Antep has more pistachio and less sugar than the Istanbul style. Buy it fresh from the maker, not from a box. Imam Çagdas and Güllüoglu are the two names to know.

Breakfast Soup
Beyran

Gaziantep's answer to breakfast: a thin broth of slow-cooked lamb, rice and garlic served with a serious amount of Maras red pepper. It is eaten in the morning, before 10 AM, and the best places close when they run out. Order it with extra pepper. The broth is the point, not the meat. A cup of tea follows.

Kebab
Ali Nazik

Fire-roasted aubergine puree with garlic yogurt, topped with ground lamb kebab cooked in butter and tomato paste. A regional classic that originated in the southeastern kitchen. The smokiness of the aubergine carries the whole dish. Eaten with thin flatbread and raw onion. Found at any proper kebab house in the old city.

Rice Dish
Mumbar Dolması

Lamb intestine stuffed with a spiced rice and herb filling, then slowly simmered in a tomato and butter sauce. A traditional Antep dish rarely found outside the city. Available in most lokanta-style restaurants in the bazaar area at lunchtime. Order it as a main and share between two.

Pastry
Katmer

Thin dough layered with kaymak (clotted cream) and coarsely ground pistachio, cooked on a copper plate. A morning food, served warm immediately off the griddle. The Antep version uses more pistachio than cream and less sugar than elsewhere. Best between 07:00 and 10:00. After that, the dough sits and loses its texture.

Dessert
Künefe

Shredded wheat pastry filled with fresh string cheese, cooked in butter until golden and crispy, then soaked in syrup and finished with crushed pistachio. Served immediately and eaten hot. The contrast between the crispy pastry, soft cheese and cold syrup is the thing. Order one between two as a shared dessert after lunch.

Where to Eat

Recommended Restaurants

Ratings based on Google reviews. Concentration of top options is highest around the castle and old bazaar area.

Imam Çagdas
★★★★★ 4.7 · 3,800+ reviews

The most famous baklava maker in Antep, operating since 1887 from the same location near the old bazaar. The baklava here is made fresh daily and sold by weight. The shop also serves a full menu of kebabs and local dishes at lunch. Arrive early, it fills up fast on weekends.

Antep Baklava, Kebab, Katmer
Yüzevler Beyran
★★★★★ 4.6 · 1,200+ reviews

A beyran specialist that has been open since early morning for decades. The broth is prepared overnight and the restaurant often runs out by mid-morning on busy days. No menu beyond beyran and bread. Cash only. The interior is simple and the service fast. Go before 09:00 for the best experience.

Beyran, Bread, Tea
Tahmis Kahvesi
★★★★★ 4.6 · 900+ reviews

One of the oldest coffee houses in the region, operating in the old bazaar for centuries. Known for its Antep-style menengiç coffee, made from the berries of wild pistachio trees rather than coffee beans. The setting is historic, the crowd is local, and the coffee is worth the visit on its own.

Menengiç Kahvesi, Çay, Mırra
Çulcular Han Kebap
★★★★☆ 4.5 · 700+ reviews

A well-established kebab restaurant in a converted Ottoman han near the castle. The Ali Nazik and the lahmacun are the two things to order. The courtyard setting in summer is pleasant. Portions are generous and prices are reasonable by any standard. Good option for a proper sit-down lunch after the Zeugma.

Experiences

Things to Do

01
A Morning in the Old City

Start with beyran before 09:00, then walk to the castle for the views and the Heroism Museum, continue through the copper bazaar while the craftsmen are working, and finish at the Zincirli Bedesten for spices and dried fruit. This route takes about 3 hours at a relaxed pace and covers the core of historic Antep without rushing.

02
Full Day at Zeugma

Arrive at opening (08:30) and budget three hours minimum. Get the audio guide at the ticket desk. Block A holds the Zeugma mosaics including the Gypsy Girl and the Mars statue. The bridge to Block B leads to church floor mosaics from the Byzantine period. The museum cafe is decent for lunch. Return in the afternoon for the baklava tour near Imam Çagdas.

03
Baklava Tour

Walk the stretch between Imam Çagdas and Güllüoglu in the old bazaar area, tasting baklava from each. The differences in pistachio quality, dough thickness and syrup ratio are real and noticeable. Buy by weight, not by box. Ask for it fresh rather than from the display tray. The best time is between 10:00 and 12:00 when the morning bake is still warm.

Beyond the City

Day Trips

85 km northeast
Halfeti and Rumkale

The drowned village of Halfeti, partially submerged since the Birecik Dam flooded the valley, sits on the Euphrates with old stone houses still emerging from the water at low level. From here, take a boat to Rumkale. The round trip from Gaziantep takes a full day. The drive through the river valley is part of the experience.

95 km west
Sanliurfa

The city of prophets is a 90-minute drive east on the D400. Balıklıgöl, the ancient castle, the covered bazaar and Gobeklitepe nearby make it a full day. The two cities together form one of the most historically dense routes in the country. Many travelers do Gaziantep and Sanliurfa as a two-city combination over four days.
