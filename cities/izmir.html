Why Visit

Izmir

Izmir is the city that gets overlooked, and the people who live there are quietly glad about it. Turkey's third largest city sits right on the Gulf of Izmir, open to the Aegean, with a waterfront that locals use every day rather than save for tourists. The Kordon promenade at sunset, packed with people walking, cycling and drinking tea within earshot of the sea, tells you more about Izmir in ten minutes than any guidebook can.

The city has been inhabited for over 8,500 years, from ancient Smyrna through Greek, Roman, Byzantine and Ottoman periods. That long history sits inside modern neighborhoods. The Agora of Smyrna is hemmed in by apartment buildings and still actively excavated. Kadifekale castle looks over the whole city from its hill. Kemeraltı Bazaar has been operating continuously since the 17th century. The layers are visible if you look.

What Izmir does differently is the pace. Aegean means relaxed, late evenings, good food taken seriously and a general disinterest in rushing. It is also one of the best bases in Turkey for day trips, sitting within easy reach of Ephesus, Pergamon, Cesme and Sirince.

Places to Visit

01. Kadifekale
Kadifekale

The castle that built the city. Constructed in the 3rd century BC by Lysimachos, a general of Alexander the Great, it sits 186 metres above Izmir on Mount Pagos. Go by taxi in daylight, walk the medieval walls, look at the Roman cisterns below. The view over the Gulf and down into the city is the reason to come. No entrance fee, rarely crowded.

02. Agora of Smyrna
Agora of Smyrna

A Roman agora in the middle of a living city. The original Hellenistic marketplace was destroyed by an earthquake in 178 AD and rebuilt under Emperor Marcus Aurelius. You walk between colonnades that are still being excavated today, with apartment buildings visible over the walls. Open daily 08:30 to 19:00. A few euros entry, very much worth it.

03. Kemeraltı Bazaar
Kemeraltı Bazaar

One of the oldest continuously operating markets in Turkey, running since the 17th century. Inside, the Kizlaragasi Han, an 18th-century Ottoman caravanserai built in 1744, is a good place to stop for coffee in a stone courtyard. Havra Sokak (Synagogue Street) nearby is a quieter part worth finding. Go on a weekday morning before the heat builds.

04. Konak Square and Clock Tower
Konak Square and Clock Tower

The geographic center of Izmir. The clock tower dates to 1901, designed by a Levantine French architect to mark the 25th anniversary of Sultan Abdulhamid II's reign. Konak Pier next to it was originally designed by Gustave Eiffel in the 1890s as a customs house, now a waterfront dining and shopping complex. Good tram connections north to Alsancak from here.

05. Kordon and Alsancak
Kordon and Alsancak

The Kordon promenade runs along the Aegean waterfront north through Alsancak. In the early evening it fills with locals walking, cycling and sitting outside at seafood restaurants and tea gardens. Alsancak itself is the neighborhood of old Greek townhouses now turned into bars and cafes. This is where Izmir is most itself. Come after 18:00.

06. Asansör
Asansör

A historic lift built in 1907 by Jewish banker Nesim Levi Bayraklioglu to connect two levels of the Karatas neighborhood. Today it is a free ride to a terrace with one of Izmir's best views over the bay. The restaurant at the top is good for a sunset meal. From Konak Pier it is about a 30-minute walk along the water, or take the tram.

Food and Cuisine

Izmir's food is Aegean through and through: olive oil, fresh herbs, seafood, vegetables and a lightness that distinguishes it from the heavier cuisines of central or eastern Turkiye. The city has its own dishes that are hard to find elsewhere, and a breakfast culture that locals treat as sacred.

The Morning Ritual
Boyoz

A flaky, slightly oily pastry with Sephardic Jewish origins, boyoz has been baked in Izmir since the early 20th century. The dough is plain, the texture is layered, and it is eaten for breakfast with a boiled egg and tea. The good bakeries in Kemeraltı open early and sell out by mid-morning.

Aegean Sandwich
Kumru

Izmir's answer to the sandwich. A soft, round bread is filled with sucuk sausage, salami, kashar cheese, tomato and pickles. The original version comes from Cesme, about 80 km west, but Izmir has absorbed it entirely. Every good snack spot in the city has a version worth trying.

The Evening Table
Seafood Meze

Dinner at a good Izmir seafood restaurant starts with a table of cold mezes: octopus, sea bass carpaccio, stuffed mussels, marinated anchovies. Then grilled fish, wine and more time than you planned. This ritual is clearest on the Kordon and in Alsancak, where the restaurants have been doing it the same way for decades.

Local Staple
Izmir Kofte

Not to be confused with any other city's version. Izmir kofte are small, elongated meatballs cooked in a tomato sauce with potatoes, served in the pan they were cooked in. Found in lokantas throughout the city. This is mid-week food, not special occasion food. Order the local bread on the side.

Aegean Tradition
Zeytinyagli

The Aegean region produces some of the best olive oil in Turkey and Izmir's cuisine is built on it. Zeytinyagli dishes, vegetables cooked slowly in olive oil and served at room temperature, appear in every lokanta. Artichoke with lemon, green beans with tomato, stuffed peppers. Seasonal, simple and consistently good.

Weekend Morning
Aegean Breakfast

Izmir's breakfast spread takes itself seriously. Local olives, aged white cheese from Aegean villages, fresh tomatoes, eggs, honey and bread. Many cafes in Alsancak and around the bazaar serve extended versions of this on weekend mornings. Arrive after 09:00 when the light is good and the crowds have not yet built.

On the Ground

Activities and Experiences

01
A Full Day in Konak

Start at Kadifekale in the morning when it is cool and the view is clear. Walk down into Kemeraltı Bazaar, find the Kizlaragasi Han for coffee in the courtyard, spend an hour at the Agora. End at Konak Square by the waterfront. The whole loop takes a full day and covers 8,500 years of the same city without taking a bus.

02
Evening on the Kordon

Take the tram north to Alsancak and walk the Kordon promenade back south as the light changes over the Gulf. Stop at one of the seafood restaurants for meze and raki as the evening begins. This is the Izmir ritual and it works every time. Summer evenings here run until midnight easily without any effort.

03
Ferry to Karsiyaka

Take the public ferry from Konak across the Gulf to Karsiyaka, a neighborhood on the northern shore that most visitors never reach. The crossing takes about 20 minutes and the view back toward the city is one of the best in Izmir. Karsiyaka's waterfront has good fish restaurants and a completely local atmosphere.

Further Afield

Day Trips from Izmir

80 km South, about 1 hour
Ephesus and Sirince

Ephesus (Efes) is one of the best-preserved Roman cities in the world. Go before 09:00, when the tour groups have not yet arrived. After Ephesus, the village of Sirince is 8 km uphill: stone houses, wine, local fruit preserves and a completely different rhythm from the ruins below. The two together fill a very good day.

80 km West, about 1 hour 15 min
Cesme and Alacati

Cesme is the peninsula at the end of the Izmir Gulf, known for its Aegean beaches and the Ottoman castle by the port. Alacati, 10 km south of Cesme, is a stone-paved village of boutique hotels and wine bars. The windmills and cobblestone streets photograph well in the afternoon light. Both are worth a full day together.
