Why Visit

Ankara

Ankara is consistently underestimated as a destination and this works in the favour of people who choose it. It has no sea and no dramatic natural setting. What it has is the full apparatus of a capital city: the finest collection of Anatolian antiquities in the world, a mausoleum that functions as a place of genuine national pilgrimage, a castle district that preserves a fragment of old Ankara within the modern city, and a dining scene that operates at a very high level without Istanbul's prices or crowds.

The Museum of Anatolian Civilizations holds Neolithic finds from Catalhoyuk, the full spectrum of Hittite art and artifacts, Phrygian, Urartian and early Lydian pieces. It has been named European Museum of the Year. It is, without qualification, one of the best museums in Turkey and among the most important in the world for this period of human history. A serious visit requires half a day.

Anitkabir, the mausoleum of Mustafa Kemal Ataturk, is one of the most charged spaces in modern Turkey. The ceremonial plaza, the Hall of Honor, and the guard of honour all contribute to an experience that is more than architectural. For any visitor who wants to understand the Republic of Turkey and how it understands itself, this is the required starting point.

Places to Visit

01. Anitkabir
Anitkabir

The mausoleum of Mustafa Kemal Ataturk, founder of the Turkish Republic, built on a hill in the Cankaya district and opened in 1953. The complex includes the Road of Lions, the Ceremonial Plaza, the Hall of Honor containing the marble sarcophagus, and a comprehensive museum on the War of Independence and the early republic. Entry is free. The ceremony of the changing of the guard operates on a regular schedule. Allow two hours minimum. Closed Mondays.

02. Ankara Castle (Kalesi)
Ankara Castle

A hilltop citadel with foundations from the 8th century BC, rebuilt successively by Galatians, Romans, Byzantines, Seljuks and Ottomans. The inner castle walls and towers are largely intact. The old neighbourhood within and around the walls, called Atpazari, has narrow lanes, Ottoman wooden houses and small craft workshops. The view from the highest tower over the modern city below is the best in Ankara. The castle area is also the location of the Museum of Anatolian Civilizations directly below its southern walls.

03. Museum of Anatolian Civilizations
Museum of Anatolian Civilizations

Housed in a restored 15th-century Ottoman bazaar building at the base of Ankara Castle, this museum holds the most complete collection of Anatolian prehistoric and ancient artifacts anywhere in the world. Neolithic figurines from Catalhoyuk, Hittite reliefs and steles, Phrygian metalwork, Urartian bronzes, early Lydian pieces. Named European Museum of the Year in 1997. Allow at least three hours. The building itself, with its domed storage chambers and courtyard, is worth visiting independently of the collection.

04. Kocatepe Mosque
Kocatepe Mosque

The largest mosque in Ankara, completed in 1987 after 25 years of construction. Built in a neo-Ottoman style with four minarets and a large central dome, it occupies a prominent position on a hill in the Kizilay district visible from much of the city. The interior holds about 24,000 worshippers and features Iznik-inspired tile panels and calligraphy. Entry is free outside prayer times. The basement contains a shopping centre, a detail that surprises most first-time visitors.

05. Kizilay and Kavaklidere
Kizilay and Kavaklidere

Kizilay is the commercial and transport centre of modern Ankara, and Kavaklidere to its south is where the embassies, international hotels and the better restaurants are concentrated. The Tunali Hilmi street in Kavaklidere is the main dining and cafe corridor. The level of restaurant quality in this neighbourhood is high and consistently underrated compared to Istanbul. The meyhane tradition here is quieter and more focused on the food rather than the atmosphere.

06. Temple of Augustus and Haci Bayram Mosque
Temple of Augustus

The Temple of Augustus in Ankara, built around 25 BC, contains the most complete text of the Res Gestae Divi Augusti, the official record of Emperor Augustus's achievements, carved on the interior walls. The Ottoman Haci Bayram Mosque from the 15th century stands directly against one of the temple walls in an unusually close juxtaposition of Roman and Ottoman architecture. Located in the old Ulus district, 500 metres northwest of Ankara Castle.

Food and Cuisine

Ankara's food scene is not organised around a single famous dish or regional tradition in the way that Gaziantep or the Black Sea coast is. What it has is a broad, capital-city food culture drawing on Central Anatolian ingredients and the presence of a large, internationally connected population that demands quality. The tavuk gogsu from the Ottoman palace tradition, the Central Anatolian lamb dishes, and the kebab restaurants in the Ulus area are the local specifics.

The Regional
Ankara Tava

A slow-cooked lamb casserole specific to the Ankara region, made with chunks of lamb on the bone cooked in a clay vessel with onions, tomatoes and peppers until the meat falls away. It is a dish that requires patience in the preparation and time in the eating. Found at traditional Central Anatolian restaurants in the Ulus district and in the old market area near Ankara Castle. Order it when you see it on a daily menu, as it is not always available.

Palace Dessert
Tavuk Gogsu

A milk pudding made with shredded chicken breast, rice flour and milk, served cold with cinnamon. It sounds improbable but is a genuinely good dessert. The chicken is entirely flavourless in the final preparation and contributes only texture. It dates from the Ottoman palace kitchens. The Ankara version tends to be less sweet than the Istanbul version. Available at traditional patisseries (pastane) and good lokanta across the city.

Central Anatolia
Kuru Fasulye

Slow-cooked white beans in a tomato and lamb stock base, eaten with rice and pickled vegetables. It is the reference dish of the Central Anatolian lokanta tradition and a staple at any self-respecting lunch counter in Ankara. The best versions have been cooking since morning. It is consumed at lunch, accompanied by a slice of white bread and occasionally a pickled pepper. Inexpensive, filling and well-suited to the cold Ankara winters.

The Classic
Lamb Kebab

Central Anatolia produces good lamb on the high plateau and the kebab tradition in Ankara reflects this. The standard is Urfa kebab, mixed kebab on a lavash base, and the lamb shish at quality kebab restaurants in Ulus and the old city. The meat is prepared simply and the accompaniments are secondary. For the better versions, look at the restaurants that have been operating in the same spot for twenty or more years and whose lunch service is full of government workers.

Pastry Culture
Corek and Pastas

The patisserie and pastry culture in Ankara is considerably stronger than in most Turkish cities outside Istanbul. Kavaklidere and Cankaya have several high-quality patisseries that produce good lokum, baklava, and the various corek pastries of the Central Anatolian tradition. The Ankara-specific pastry tradition includes the acikali pogaca, a savoury cheese-filled dough roll eaten for breakfast. Buy from a local neighbourhood bakery rather than a chain.

Meyhane
Kavaklidere Meyhane

The meyhane tradition in Ankara is centred on the Kavaklidere and Gaziosmanpasa districts. It is more subdued than Beyoglu in Istanbul and more focused on the food. The meze selection at good Ankara meyhanes includes a wider range of Central Anatolian preparations alongside the standard cold dishes. Raki service follows the same ritual as elsewhere in Turkey. The evenings are quieter and the conversations last longer.

Trilye Restaurant
★★★★★ 4.6 (780+ reviews)

A fish and meze restaurant in Kavaklidere known for its quality produce and consistent kitchen. The Aegean-influenced meze selection is good and the daily fish comes from the coast. One of the references for serious dining in Ankara outside the hotel restaurants.

Aegean fish, Mezes, Regional wines
Hatipler Et Lokantasi
★★★★★ 4.6 (650+ reviews)

A long-standing meat restaurant in the Ulus area near the castle, known for its daily lamb preparations and the Ankara tava. The lunch service is local and efficient. The evening is quieter and the portions generous. One of the references for Central Anatolian meat cooking in the city.

Ankara Tava, Lamb kebab, Daily specials
Ugrak Lokantasi
★★★★★ 4.5 (520+ reviews)

A traditional lokanta near Kizilay that serves daily home-style cooking at honest prices. The kuru fasulye, the lamb stew and the stuffed vegetables are the standard orders. Busy at lunch with civil servants and university staff. Closed evenings and weekends.

Kuru fasulye, Daily stews, Home cooking
On the Ground

Activities and Experiences

01
Museum of Anatolian Civilizations

Allocate a full morning. Buy the audio guide. The Neolithic room comes first and establishes the timeline. The Chalcolithic transition, then the Bronze Age. The Hittite gallery is the centrepiece. The Phrygian room includes the intact furniture from the royal tomb at Gordion, one of the most intact Bronze Age furnishings in existence. Do not rush the Urartian bronzes. This museum rewards the people who read the labels and move slowly.

02
Ankara Castle District Walk

Enter Atpazari through the main gate of the castle and walk the inner walls. The neighbourhood inside has workshops making traditional crafts, small restaurants in old stone buildings and several good tea houses. Exit through the eastern gate and walk down through the old Ulus neighbourhood past the Haci Bayram Mosque and the Temple of Augustus. Allow three hours and stop for lunch in the castle area before descending. Best done on a weekday when the workshops are open.

03
Anitkabir at Opening

The mausoleum opens at 9am and the first hour sees far fewer visitors than later in the day. The changing of the guard ceremony happens at regular intervals and is worth timing your visit around. Walk the Road of Lions at the entrance slowly. The museum in the complex is comprehensive and covers the War of Independence in detail with period photographs, documents and personal effects of Ataturk. The Hall of Honor, where the sarcophagus is located, requires silence and is enforced as such.

Further Afield

Day Trips

90 km West, about 1 hour 15 min
Gordion

The ancient Phrygian capital of Gordion, site of the legendary Gordian Knot that Alexander the Great cut with his sword in 333 BC. The burial tumuli of Phrygian kings are visible on the landscape. The largest, the Tumulus MM, is attributed to King Midas and contains intact wooden tomb furniture from the 8th century BC now housed in the Museum of Anatolian Civilizations. A small on-site museum provides context. Best combined with an early departure and return by afternoon.

150 km East, about 2 hours
Hattusa

The capital of the Hittite Empire from approximately 1650 to 1200 BC, a UNESCO World Heritage Site near the town of Bogazkoy. The city walls, the Great Temple, the royal palace mound and the rock sanctuary of Yazilikaya with its carved reliefs are the main sites. The scale of the ruins and the age of the civilisation they represent makes this one of the more affecting archaeological sites in Anatolia. Allow a full day with an early start from Ankara.
