Why Visit

Afyonkarahisar

Afyonkarahisar is one of those Turkish cities most travelers pass through without stopping. The ones who do stop tend to stay longer than planned. The volcanic black rock rising 226 metres above the city plain, with a Hittite-era castle at its summit and 700 steps to reach it, dominates the landscape and the psychology of the place. The old neighborhoods at its base — cobbled lanes, timber-framed Ottoman houses, and Seljuk mosques with carved wooden interiors — are among the most intact historic urban fabric in western Anatolia.

The city is Turkey's undisputed capital of thermal tourism. The springs at Ömer, Gazlıgöl, Gecek, Heybeli and Hüdai reach temperatures between 46 and 85 degrees and have been drawing visitors since antiquity. The 5-star thermal hotel concentration here is higher than anywhere else in Turkey — purpose-built complexes with full medical spa facilities, not just pools. A weekend here, properly spent between the historic centre and a thermal resort, is genuinely restorative.

And then there is the food. Afyon kaymak — clotted cream served on warm lokma or with honey and fresh bread at a morning tea house — is in a category of its own. The sucuk produced here has an appellation of origin. These are not minor regional variations. They are the real things, and eating them in Afyon at a table by an old stone wall in the morning is the kind of experience that stays with you.

Places to Visit

01. Afyonkarahisar Castle
Afyonkarahisar Kalesi

A Hittite fortification from around 1350 BC, perched on a 226-metre volcanic rock above the city. The climb requires 700 steps on a well-maintained stone staircase from the south side and takes about 30 minutes at a steady pace. From the summit the view extends across the plateau in every direction. The castle walls, cisterns and rock-cut chambers are free to enter. The physical effort of the ascent, and the city spread below when you arrive, are the visit. Best in the early morning before the heat of the day and before the crowds.

02. Ulu Cami
Afyonkarahisar Ulu Camii

Built in 1272 under Seljuk rule, this mosque is one of the finest surviving examples of Anatolian wooden architecture from the period. Forty wooden columns support a flat timber-beam ceiling with carved capitals in stalactite decoration — no nails were used in the original construction. The interior has the feeling of a forest made sacred. Located at the foot of the castle rock in the old quarter. Entry is free outside prayer times. The mosque is on the UNESCO Tentative World Heritage List.

03. Gedik Ahmet Pasa Camii
Gedik Ahmet Pasa Complex

Built in 1472 by Gedik Ahmet Pasha, vizier of Mehmed the Conqueror, this is the most significant Ottoman monument in Afyon. The mosque has an inverted-T plan with two consecutive domes and a single minaret decorated with blue-glazed spiral fluting, an unusual detail worth examining closely. The complex includes a medrese and an attached hamam (the Imaret Baths) that still operates with original stone basins. Located at the southern end of Birlik Caddesi, a 5-minute walk from the castle.

04. Zafer Muzesi and Old Town
Victory Museum and Historic Quarters

Afyon's Victory Museum documents the Turkish War of Independence and the Great Offensive of August 1922, in which Ataturk launched the decisive counterattack from Kocatepe hill. The museum occupies the Hacivelioglu Mansion where the final battle orders were given. The cobbled lanes of the old quarter surrounding the castle base contain surviving Ottoman timber-framed houses and several small antique shops and tea houses. The Mevlevi Lodge nearby is the second most important after Konya's, founded in the 13th century.

05. Omer Thermal Springs
Ömer Ilıcası

Located 15 km north of the city centre on the Kütahya road, Ömer is the most developed of Afyon's four main thermal districts. The springs reach 46 to 71 degrees and contain sodium chloride and bicarbonate with known therapeutic properties for joint and skin conditions. Several 5-star thermal hotels operate here with indoor and outdoor pools, spa facilities and medical consultation services. Day-use access is available at most hotels for a fee. The setting in a quiet valley is noticeably calm.

06. Kocatepe Aniti
Kocatepe Monument and National Park

The hill from which Ataturk personally commanded the opening of the Great Offensive on 26 August 1922, the decisive military action of the Turkish War of Independence. The well-known photograph of Ataturk looking out over the plain was taken here. A monument and the original observation trenches remain at the site, within the Commander-in-Chief Historical National Park that spans Afyon, Kutahya and Usak provinces. 15 km west of the city centre. Free entry. The panoramic view of the Anatolian plateau from the hilltop is significant.

07. İhsaniye Phrygian Valley
İhsaniye Frigya Vadisi

The Phrygian Valley east of Afyonkarahisar contains the most concentrated collection of Phrygian rock monuments in Turkey — carved facades, stepped altars and the Midas Monument, a 17-metre-high rock relief from the 8th century BC. The valley landscape of volcanic tufa formations and open steppe gives the monuments an otherworldly setting. Best visited on a full day from the city with a vehicle.

08. Dinar Apameia
Apameia Antik Kenti

The ancient Hellenistic and Roman city of Apameia (modern Dinar), 90 km south of Afyonkarahisar, with a well-preserved colonnaded street, a nymphaeum and temple remains from the 2nd century AD. The Dinar Archaeological Museum houses finds from the site. The Dinar plain and the source of the Meander River nearby add a landscape dimension to the archaeological visit.

Local Cuisine

What to Eat

Afyon has two foods with genuine national standing: kaymak and sucuk. The kaymak here — slow-skimmed buffalo cream, thick and slightly yellow — is a different product from what is sold under the same name elsewhere in Turkey. The sucuk has a protected geographical indication. These two alone justify a stop. The broader food culture is Central Anatolian: lamb, legumes, flatbreads from wood-fired ovens, and a straightforward breakfast culture that takes these ingredients seriously.

The Signature
Afyon Kaymak

The defining food of Afyonkarahisar, made from the slow-skimmed cream of water buffalo milk. The buffalo graze on the mineral-rich highland pastures around the city and their milk produces a cream denser, richer and more complex in flavour than the cow-milk kaymak found elsewhere. It is served on warm lokma (fried dough) drizzled with honey, or spread thick on fresh bread at breakfast. The dedicated kaymak houses around the city serve it from the morning until it sells out. Order it with the simplest accompaniments.

Protected Origin
Afyon Sucuk

A dry-cured spiced sausage with a Protected Geographical Indication, made from a blend of beef and lamb with cumin, garlic and black pepper in ratios specific to this region. The curing process and the local climate produce a sucuk with a firmer texture and more pronounced spice than commercial alternatives. Sold throughout Turkey but genuinely different when bought from producers in Afyon. Best eaten pan-fried and served with eggs and fresh bread at breakfast, or sliced cold as a meze.

Morning Table
Afyon Kahvaltısı

The Afyon breakfast is organized around kaymak and sucuk, but a full table adds local white cheese, several varieties of olives, honey from highland beehives, boiled eggs, fresh tomatoes and cucumbers, and bread from wood-fired ovens still operating in the city's older neighbourhoods. Served with tea in the standard tulip glass. The better breakfast houses near the castle base serve genuine local produce. Allow an unhurried hour for this meal — it rewards the time.

Central Anatolia
Afyon Tandir Kebabi

Lamb slow-cooked in a sealed clay pit oven — the tandır — until the meat separates from the bone without effort. The technique is Central Anatolian and Afyon's version uses plateau lamb with a higher fat content that keeps the meat moist through the long cooking time. The result is rich, falling apart and deeply flavoured. Served with flatbread, roasted vegetables and plain yogurt. Found at the traditional lokantas in the city centre and in the older bazaar area. Order at lunch when it has been cooking since early morning.

The Sweet
Afyon Lokması

Small fried dough pieces served warm with kaymak and honey poured over them at the table. The lokma is the canonical vehicle for Afyon kaymak and the combination — hot dough, cold cream, dark honey — is one of the great simple food experiences of Turkish cuisine. Available at the dedicated kaymak houses in the city, served from the morning. The quality of the lokma (crisp outside, soft inside) varies between establishments; find one where they are made continuously rather than sitting warm.

Regional Classic
Haşhaş Ekmegi

Poppy seed bread, a logical product of the region that has historically been one of the world's largest producers of pharmaceutical opium poppies. The seeds — which carry no narcotic content — give a nutty, slightly bitter flavour to flatbreads and simit-style rings. Sold at bakeries throughout the city and at the bazaar. Afyon province remains Turkey's largest producer of legally cultivated opium poppies for the pharmaceutical industry, a fact the city wears without embarrassment — it is in the name.

Recommended Restaurants

Experiences

Things to Do

01
Climb the Castle at Dawn

The 700-step ascent to Afyonkarahisar Castle takes about 25 to 30 minutes on the maintained stone staircase from the south side. Starting before sunrise means arriving at the summit as the plateau catches the first light — the view across the flat Anatolian landscape in every direction at that hour is the best argument for visiting Afyon at all. The castle is free to enter and never crowded in the early morning. Bring water and wear shoes with grip. The descent is harder than the ascent on the knees.

02
A Day at a Thermal Resort

Afyon's 5-star thermal hotels at Ömer and Gazlıgöl offer day-use access to pools and spa facilities for a set fee, typically between 300 and 600 TL. The thermal water at these springs is genuinely mineral-rich and the effect after three or four hours in the pools is perceptible. Most hotels have both indoor pools at a consistent 38 degrees and outdoor pools that vary. Weekdays are significantly quieter than weekends. Book day-use in advance during summer months. The experience is markedly different from standard hotel pools.

03
Old Quarter and Bazaar Walk

The cobbled lanes around the base of the castle rock hold the city's historic character: the Ulu Cami, the Gedik Ahmet Pasha Complex with its working hamam, the Mevlevi Lodge museum, and a concentration of Ottoman timber-framed houses. The covered bazaar nearby sells Afyon sucuk, dried herbs, marble products and locally made textiles. Allow two to three hours to walk slowly through this area, stopping at a tea house in a courtyard when one presents itself. The neighborhood is compact and walkable from the castle base.

From Afyon

Day Trips

120 km south, about 1 hr 30 min
Pamukkale and Hierapolis

The white travertine terraces of Pamukkale and the Greco-Roman city of Hierapolis above them are a viable day trip from Afyon on the D565 south. The drive through the Meander Valley is straightforward. Arrive at the South Gate before 08:00, walk the terraces early, then explore Hierapolis with its theater and necropolis before the heat peaks. The Hierapolis Archaeology Museum is included in the entry ticket. Return to Afyon in the early evening. A long but manageable day.

55 km northwest, about 1 hr
Phrygian Valley

The Phrygian Valley in the Ihsaniye district, 35 km north of Afyon, is a landscape of volcanic rock formations — fairy chimneys, carved rock churches, Phrygian tombs and early Christian cave dwellings — that rival Cappadocia in geological character while receiving a fraction of the visitors. The Yazılıkaya Phrygian monument, Aslankaya relief and the rock-cut valley settlements are the highlights. A half-day circuit by car from Afyon city centre covers the main sites. Rarely visited by foreign tourists.
