Why Visit

Istanbul

Istanbul is the only city in the world that sits across two continents, and this is not merely a geographic curiosity. It shapes everything: the food, the architecture, the pace of the streets, the way the city thinks about itself. The European side carries the weight of Byzantium and Ottoman imperial ambition. The Asian side is quieter, more residential, a place where Istanbullus go when they want to eat well without crowds. The Bosphorus between them is a working strait, and watching the tankers pass at dusk from a waterfront tea house on the Asian shore is one of the better ways to understand the scale of this place.

Sultanahmet is where the Byzantine and Ottoman empires built their great monuments within walking distance of each other. Hagia Sophia, completed in 537 AD, held the record as the largest cathedral in the world for almost a thousand years. The Blue Mosque directly opposite was built in the 17th century by Ottoman architects who understood exactly what they were competing with. Topkapi Palace to the northeast was the administrative centre of an empire that stretched from the Danube to the Persian Gulf. You can walk between all three in under ten minutes.

Beyond Sultanahmet, Istanbul rewards patience. Karakoy and Beyoglu north of the Golden Horn have the galleries, fish restaurants and meyhane culture. Kadikoy on the Asian side has the best produce market and the most honest street food. The Bosphorus ferry crossing on a public boat for a few lira is one of the great city experiences anywhere.

Places to Visit

01. Hagia Sophia
Hagia Sophia

Built between 532 and 537 AD under Emperor Justinian I, Hagia Sophia was the largest cathedral in the world for nearly a thousand years. Its main dome, 55 metres high and 31 metres in diameter, appears to float. The interior held Byzantine mosaics over more than a millennium. It became a mosque in 1453 after the Ottoman conquest and was reconverted to mosque status in 2020. Entry is free. Go in the first hour it opens at 9am, before the crowds arrive. The light through the upper windows in the morning is remarkable.

02. Blue Mosque (Sultanahmet)
Blue Mosque

Built between 1609 and 1616 by architect Sedefkar Mehmed Agha for Sultan Ahmed I, the Sultanahmet Mosque is named for the 20,000 hand-painted Iznik tiles lining its interior. It is the only Ottoman imperial mosque in Istanbul with six minarets. The architect was reported to have been told to build gold minarets, misheard it as six. The mosque remains active for daily prayers. Entry is free but the mosque closes to visitors during the five daily prayer times. Check current hours before visiting.

03. Topkapi Palace
Topkapi Palace

The administrative heart of the Ottoman Empire from the 1460s until the 1850s, Topkapi occupies the tip of the historic peninsula overlooking both the Bosphorus and the Golden Horn. The palace complex holds the Imperial Treasury with the Topkapi Dagger, the throne rooms, the Harem quarters and a collection of Islamic relics including the mantle of the Prophet. Allow a full day. Buy tickets in advance. Closed on Tuesdays.

04. Basilica Cistern
Basilica Cistern

Built in 532 AD under Justinian I to supply water to the Great Palace of Constantinople, the Basilica Cistern holds 80,000 cubic metres of water supported by 336 marble columns. Two columns at the far end stand on carved Medusa heads, one upside down and one sideways. The cistern appeared briefly in the James Bond film From Russia with Love. It was extensively renovated and reopened in 2022 with new lighting and walkways. Walk slowly through it. The acoustics and the reflections in the water justify the visit.

05. Galata Tower
Galata Tower

A medieval stone tower built by the Genoese in 1348 at 66.9 metres, sitting at the highest point of the Galata district north of the Golden Horn. The view from the top balcony takes in the entire Sultanahmet skyline, the Bosphorus and the Asian shore. The queue for the lift can be long at weekends and in summer. Going early in the morning or at dusk avoids the worst of it. The surrounding Galata neighbourhood has the best coffee shops and independent bookstores in the city.

06. Grand Bazaar
Grand Bazaar

One of the oldest and largest covered markets in the world, with 61 covered streets and around 4,000 shops. First established in 1455 after the Ottoman conquest, it has been operating continuously ever since. The Bedesten at the centre is the oldest part and sells the best antiques. The outer lanes are tourist-facing. Go on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning when it is quieter. Do not make any purchase without a firm price negotiation. The bazaar closes on Sundays.

Food and Cuisine

Istanbul has every food tradition that has passed through this city over two thousand years. The result is a depth of food culture that rewards time spent eating methodically across different neighbourhoods. The Kadikoy market on the Asian side is the place to understand what local Istanbullus actually eat. The meyhane dinner in Beyoglu is the social institution. The balik ekmek at Galata Bridge is the democracy.

The Icon
Balik Ekmek

A grilled mackerel fillet in a half-loaf of bread with onion, lettuce and lemon, sold from boats moored at the Galata Bridge and from small stands along the Golden Horn. It is one of the great pieces of street food in any city and costs almost nothing. The fishermen on the bridge above are catching the same mackerel the stands sell below. Eat it standing at the water. This is the correct way.

The Classic
Iskender Kebab

Thin slices of doner meat laid over pieces of pide bread, covered with tomato sauce and hot butter, served with yogurt. Invented in Bursa in 1867 by Iskender Efendi and still made best in Bursa, but the Istanbul versions are perfectly good. Available at most proper kebab restaurants across the city. Order it for lunch rather than dinner. Eating Iskender after 9pm is a reliable way to sleep badly.

The Institution
Meze and Raki

The meyhane is the Turkish tavern tradition, where an evening is built around meze dishes, conversation and raki. It proceeds slowly and in a specific order: cold mezes first, then hot, then a main fish or meat course. Raki is diluted with cold water and turns milky white. The meyhane culture belongs to Beyoglu and Karakoy and is at its best on weekday evenings when the room is full but not frantic. Piyaz, arnavut cigeri and stuffed mussels are the essential cold mezes to order.

The Sweet
Baklava

Layers of paper-thin filo dough, chopped pistachios and clarified butter, soaked in syrup and cut into diamond shapes. The Gaziantep tradition produces the finest baklava in Turkey, and the two main Gaziantep-origin shops in Istanbul, Karakoy Gulluoglu and Hafiz Mustafa, set the standard. Buy by weight at the counter rather than in pre-packaged boxes. Eat the same day. Baklava deteriorates quickly once cut and should never be refrigerated.

Morning Ritual
Simit and Tea

The simit is a circular bread encrusted with sesame seeds, baked in a wood oven and sold from carts throughout Istanbul at any hour. It is inexpensive, good when fresh and the correct breakfast for anyone arriving at a ferry terminal in the early morning. Eat it with a glass of tea from a nearby tea house. The combination costs almost nothing and tastes entirely right in the specific context of an Istanbul morning by the water.

From the Sea
Fried Calamari

The Bosphorus calamari season runs from autumn through spring when squid move through the strait. Lightly battered and fried with good olive oil, served with lemon and a simple green salad. The best versions are at the smaller fish restaurants in Karakoy and Eminonu near the water, where the calamari was likely caught the same morning. One of those dishes that is simple to describe and very hard to find done correctly outside of a few specific places in Istanbul.

Where to Eat

Top Restaurants in Istanbul

4.5 stars and above, 500 or more reviews on Google

Ciya Sofrasi
★★★★★ 4.7 (2,800+ reviews)

A lokanta on the Asian side in Kadikoy market run by chef Musa Dagdeviren, who has spent decades documenting and cooking regional Anatolian recipes that were disappearing. The daily menu draws from dozens of regional traditions. One of the most important restaurants in Istanbul for understanding the actual breadth of Turkish food. Take the ferry across from Eminonu.

Regional Anatolian cuisine, Daily changing menu
Mikla
★★★★★ 4.6 (1,100+ reviews)

On the rooftop of the Marmara Pera hotel in Beyoglu, with one of the best views of the Bosphorus and the historic peninsula. Chef Mehmet Gurs cooks a modern Turkish menu using regional Anatolian ingredients. Book well in advance. The view at sunset is worth the price of the meal on its own.

Modern Turkish, Bosphorus views
Karakoy Lokantasi
★★★★★ 4.6 (900+ reviews)

A well-run lokanta in Karakoy with a daily menu of traditional Turkish dishes prepared with good ingredients. Busy at lunch with working Istanbullus from the surrounding neighbourhood. The fried liver, the stuffed vine leaves and the fish are consistently good. Affordable and reliable.

Daily lokanta menu, Turkish classics
Karakoy Gulluoglu
★★★★★ 4.7 (3,200+ reviews)

The oldest and most respected baklava shop in Istanbul, originating from Gaziantep and running in Karakoy since 1949. The pistachio baklava is the standard by which all other Istanbul baklava is measured. Buy by weight at the counter and eat it within the hour. Do not put it in a bag for later.

Pistachio baklava, Gaziantep pastries
Hamdi Restaurant
★★★★★ 4.5 (2,400+ reviews)

A Southeast Anatolian kebab restaurant in Eminonu near the Spice Bazaar, well regarded for its lamb kebabs and lahmacun. The terrace on the upper floor has a view of the Golden Horn. One of the more reliable places in central Istanbul for well-executed kebab in a setting where the food is the focus rather than the decor.

Lamb kebab, Lahmacun, Southeastern dishes
Balikci Sabahattin
★★★★★ 4.5 (850+ reviews)

A fish meyhane in a restored wooden house in Sultanahmet, running since 1927. The meze selection is broad and well prepared. The fish changes daily. Book in advance. One of the few genuinely good fish restaurants in the Sultanahmet area, which tends toward the tourist-facing end of the spectrum.

Seasonal fish, Cold and hot mezes, Raki
On the Ground

Activities and Experiences

01
Morning in Sultanahmet

The historic peninsula before 9am operates on a different scale. Start at Hagia Sophia when it opens. Walk to the Blue Mosque. Walk down through the Hippodrome square where the Byzantine chariot racing track still marks the ground. Descend to the Basilica Cistern. By 11am the tour groups will have arrived in force. Four monuments, no crowds, two to three hours. This sequencing is the only way to experience central Istanbul without spending most of your time waiting.

02
Bosphorus Ferry Crossing

The public ferry from Eminonu or Karakoy to Kadikoy on the Asian side runs frequently and costs very little. The 20-minute crossing gives an unobstructed view of the Sultanahmet skyline, the Topkapi promontory, the Galata Tower and the strait itself. Buy a glass of tea on board. The ferry back at dusk, looking at the minarets lit against the sky, is one of those views that is genuinely difficult to improve upon. No tour boat required.

03
Full Evening in Beyoglu

Istiklal Avenue and the streets running off it contain the meyhane tradition, the live music venues, the fish restaurants and the patisseries that make up a proper Istanbul evening. Start in Karakoy for baklava and coffee. Walk up to Galata Tower. Continue to Istiklal and into the side streets of Cihangir. Eat at a meyhane no earlier than 8pm. The evenings here run late by Turkish standards, and the correct pace is slow. This is not a neighbourhood to rush through.

Further Afield

Day Trips

Ferry from Kabatas, about 1 to 2 hours
Princes Islands

Nine islands in the Sea of Marmara, of which four are inhabited. Cars are prohibited and transport is by horse-drawn carriage or bicycle. Buyukada is the largest, with Ottoman mansions, pine forests, beach clubs and good fish restaurants. The ferry from Kabatas runs several times daily. In summer the islands are busy at weekends. Arriving on a weekday morning and leaving by mid-afternoon avoids the worst of the crowds returning to Istanbul in the evening.

160 km South, ferry from Yenikapi plus road, about 3 hours
Bursa and Uludag

The first Ottoman capital sits below the Uludag massif with its covered bazaar, the Green Mosque and Tomb from 1421, and the original Iskender kebab tradition. The fast ferry from Yenikapi to Mudanya takes about 75 minutes and is significantly more pleasant than the highway. In winter the cable car up Uludag to the ski station runs from the city centre. A one-night stay is better than a rushed day trip if time allows.
