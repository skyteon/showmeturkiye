Why Visit

Rize

Rize is the city that produces most of Turkey's tea, and the landscape shows this completely. The hillsides rising directly from the Black Sea are covered in dense, terraced tea gardens layered one above the other up into the clouds. It is one of the rainiest cities in the country, and everything about it is green. Walking through a tea garden in the morning mist is a specific kind of experience that belongs only to this part of the world.

Further inland, the landscape changes dramatically. The Firtina Valley cuts deep into the Pontic Alps, and the road following the river passes old stone arch bridges, wooden houses built into the slopes, and Zilkale Castle perched on a cliff 1,130 metres above sea level. Eventually the road climbs to Ayder Plateau at 1,350 metres, the base for exploring the Kackar Mountains. The range rises to nearly 4,000 metres and offers some of the most serious alpine trekking in Turkey.

The food culture is inseparable from the landscape. Muhlama, hamsi in season, Laz boregi from the morning pastry shops, and Anzer honey from the highland beehives. Rize rewards people who stay long enough to understand the rhythm of the place rather than passing through for a single afternoon.

Places to Visit · Eight Anchors

Where you actually go in Rize.

Eight places worth your time. Tap a photograph, the map will follow.

01

Rize Tea Gardens

The terraced tea gardens covering the hills above Rize begin practically at sea level and climb several hundred metres up into the fog. The best time to walk through them is early morning when the mist sits in the lower gardens. The picking season runs from May to October and seeing the harvest done by hand is worth planning a visit around. The Ziraat Botanic Garden in the city centre has a well-organised tea garden section and good views over the sea.

03

Ayder Plateau

At 1,350 metres in the Firtina Valley, Ayder is the main base for the Kackar Mountains and the surrounding highland plateaus. Wooden guesthouses, thermal hot springs, waterfalls within walking distance, and the Kackar ridge rising directly above. The thermal baths at the centre of the plateau use water that emerges at 55 degrees from underground. The plateau is about 88 km from Rize city centre on mountain roads, roughly 90 minutes of driving.

05

Rize Castle

A Byzantine-era hilltop fortress above the city centre with views over Rize and the Black Sea. The walls and towers have been rebuilt over the centuries but are well preserved. The climb up through the gardens takes about 15 minutes and the view from the top over the tea gardens and the sea below is worth it. Entry is free. The afternoon light falls well over the sea from this position.

07

Palovit Selalesi

A waterfall above Ayder reachable on a 3 km forest trail from the plateau. The falls drop through beech forest with volume peaking in June during snowmelt. A standard extension walk from an Ayder plateau stay.

02

Zilkale Castle

A medieval fortress built at 1,130 metres on the edge of a cliff, with the Firtina River running 380 metres below. Built by the Empire of Trebizond in the 14th or 15th century to control the mountain pass into the interior. The eight towers are largely intact. The path up from the road takes about 20 minutes and is steep. Come in the morning before the valley mist burns off for the most atmospheric light on the stone walls.

04

Firtina Valley

The Firtina River carved this deep valley through the Pontic Alps. The road running alongside it is one of the most scenic drives in the Black Sea region. Ottoman stone arch bridges appear at several points along the route, built without mortar and still standing. The river runs fast and clear. Rafting companies operate from spring through early autumn on sections suitable for beginners, and the rapids are more accessible than the route to Ayder suggests.

06

Pokut Plateau

A high plateau above Ayder, reached by a narrow road that climbs through beech and spruce forest. Traditional wooden plateau houses still stand and a small community maintains the area in summer. The views across the highland landscape toward the Kackar peaks are exceptional on clear days. It is significantly less visited than Ayder, which means quieter conditions and a more genuine plateau experience. A vehicle with good ground clearance is recommended.

08

Camlihemsin

The gateway town to the Kackar Mountains and Hemsin valleys. Stone bridges, traditional Hemsin architecture and the starting point for treks toward Ayder. The Hemsin culture and weekly market are most visible here.

Food and Cuisine

Rize has one of the most specific food cultures in Turkiye. The ingredients are local, the recipes are old, and almost nothing on a proper Rize table has arrived from elsewhere. Muhlama is the dish the region is most known for, but the anchovy season, the highland honey and the Laz boregi each have their own loyal following among people who know this coast well.

The Signature
Muhlama

Cornmeal cooked in butter with local Hemsin kolot cheese melted through it until the dish becomes thick and stringy, served bubbling in a copper pan. The Rize version uses a sharper local cheese than the Trabzon muhlama most people encounter. It is eaten for breakfast or alongside meat dishes and should be consumed while the cheese is still pulling. It cools quickly and the texture changes completely within minutes.

The Season
Hamsi

The anchovy arrives on the Black Sea in November and the season runs to April. In Rize, hamsi is pan-fried, baked into rice, pressed into cornbread and used in soups. Locals judge a restaurant by how it handles the anchovy. Visiting between November and April and ordering it at every meal is the correct approach. Outside this window the fish is frozen and the experience is entirely different from what the locals mean when they talk about hamsi.

The Pastry
Laz Boregi

Thin layers of hand-rolled dough filled with a custard cream made from eggs and milk, baked golden and finished with powdered sugar. It comes from the Laz culture of the eastern Black Sea and belongs specifically to this region. Good pastry shops sell it fresh in the morning and it is gone by noon. The version in Rize tends to be lighter in the cream filling than versions found further west along the coast.

The Ritual
Rize Tea

Rize produces approximately 75 percent of Turkey's tea. The local tea is strong, dark and full-bodied, always served in a tulip glass, never with milk. Locals drink it throughout the day. In the tea gardens above the city, small tea houses sell it by the glass for almost nothing. Sitting in one of these gardens in the morning, looking out over the sea with a fresh glass of tea, is one of the simplest and most specific pleasures this region offers.

Highland Treasure
Anzer Honey

Produced on the Anzer plateau high in the Kackar Mountains from over 450 species of wild highland flowers, Anzer honey is among the most expensive honeys in the world and one of the most complex in flavour. Sold in Rize at specialist shops and at the market. Buy from a verified producer. Counterfeits are common and easily identified by price: genuine Anzer honey commands a significant premium over standard varieties. A small jar is a worthwhile purchase.

Morning Ritual
Black Sea Breakfast

A Rize breakfast is one of the more generous you will encounter anywhere in the country. Muhlama, local cheese, Anzer honey, fresh butter, olives, eggs, cornbread and tea arrive in quantity and at an unhurried pace. Most guesthouses in Ayder serve breakfast this way and it sets the tone for the entire morning. Plan nothing in the two hours after sitting down to eat it.

Where to Eat

Top Restaurants in Rize

Serender Restaurant
★★★★★ 4.7 (800+ reviews)

One of the best-regarded lokanta in Rize city centre, known for its muhlama and Black Sea specialties. The daily menu changes with what is available locally and portions are generous. Busy at lunch with people from the market district nearby.

Muhlama, Black Sea dishes, Hamsi
Balta Restaurant
★★★★★ 4.6 (650+ reviews)

A family restaurant with a solid reputation for anchovy dishes during the November to April season. In summer the menu shifts to grilled mountain trout and Black Sea specialties. Simple, well-priced and consistent across seasons.

Hamsi Tava, Mountain Trout, Regional dishes
Ayder Yayla Restaurant
★★★★★ 4.5 (420+ reviews)

Set on the plateau itself, this restaurant serves highland food to visitors staying in Ayder. The muhlama and trout are handled well and the setting, with views across the valley, makes a long lunch entirely justified. Open from June through September only.

Muhlama, Mountain Trout, Highland breakfast
Rize Pide Salonu
★★★★★ 4.5 (380+ reviews)

A stone-oven pide specialist that has been in the same location for years. The karisik pide with cheese and egg is the standard order. Very affordable and very reliable. A good breakfast stop before heading up to the tea gardens or out toward Firtina Valley.

Karisik Pide, Pide with egg and cheese
Ayder Yayla Sofrasi
★★★★★ 4.8 · 700+ reviews

A plateau restaurant at Ayder serving Black Sea highland cooking: muhlama (cornmeal and cheese fondue), hamsi dishes, local butter and honey, and slow-cooked lamb. The most representative table of Rize-Hemsin food in a fixed setting.

Muhlama, Hamsi, Highland Table
Rize Balik Pisirme Yeri
★★★★☆ 4.6 · 500+ reviews

A fish restaurant on the Rize coast serving Black Sea catch with local cornbread. The hamsi season (November-February) is when it is at its best. Genuine coastal Black Sea table.

On the Ground

Activities and Experiences

01
Drive the Firtina Valley Road

The road from Ardesen on the coast into the Firtina Valley and up to Camlihemsin is one of the most compelling drives in the region. The valley narrows as you go deeper. Ottoman arch bridges appear over the river. The road climbs past Zilkale and continues to Ayder. Allow a full day and stop often. The light changes quickly in the valley and conditions shift from clear to misty within an hour, especially in the morning.

02
Rafting on the Firtina River

The Firtina River runs fast and cold through a narrow gorge, and guided rafting trips operate from Camlihemsin on sections suitable for beginners as well as more experienced paddlers. The season runs from April through October. Several operators in Camlihemsin rent equipment and provide guides. Advance booking is usually not necessary outside July and August weekends when demand is highest.

03
Kackar Mountains Trekking

The Kackar range rises to 3,932 metres and offers multi-day trekking routes between the Black Sea plateau system and the interior valleys. The most popular circuit connects Ayder to Yaylalar on the south side via the Dilber Duzlugu ridge. June and September offer the best conditions. July and August are busier. Mountain guides are available in Ayder and are worth hiring for anything beyond the standard plateau routes.

Further Afield

Day Trips

75 km West, about 1 hour
Trabzon and Sumela

Trabzon is an easy day trip west along the coast road with its own food culture, bazaar and the Byzantine Hagia Sophia near the sea. From Trabzon the road south into the Altindere National Park reaches Sumela Monastery in about 45 minutes. Built into a vertical cliff face in the 4th century, it is one of the most dramatic sights in the entire region and justifies the drive on its own.

50 km East, about 1 hour
Hopa and Artvin

East of Rize the coast continues to Hopa near the Georgian border. Turning inland at Hopa and climbing into the Artvin highlands leads to a completely different landscape. Deep river canyons, fortresses visible from the road and sparse, dramatic mountain scenery make for a full day well away from the tea garden aesthetic of Rize province. The drive through the Coruh River canyon is remarkable.
