Why Visit

Mardin

Mardin sits on the last ridge before the Mesopotamian plain begins, and from almost anywhere in the old city you can see as far south as Syria. The limestone buildings, all quarried from the same warm honey-coloured stone, cascade down the hillside in a continuous terrace. There is no other city in Turkey that looks quite like it from any angle.

The city has been home to Turks, Kurds, Arabs, Syriacs and Armenians for centuries, and the architecture reflects this coexistence. Mosques, Syriac churches, Armenian churches and caravanserais share the same narrow lanes. The medreseler, the old Islamic schools, are among the most elaborately carved stone buildings in the country. The old bazaar below the main road has silversmiths who have been making telkari filigree in the same spots for generations.

The Deyrulzafaran Monastery, 5 km southeast of the city, is one of the oldest continuously functioning monasteries in the world, built in 493 AD on a sun temple that predates it by two thousand years. Plan at least two full days for Mardin. Three days lets you reach Dara, Midyat and Mor Gabriel without rushing.

Places to Visit

01. Old City and Bazaar
Mardin Old City

Walk without a fixed route. Start from Cumhuriyet Caddesi and descend into the lanes below. The stone houses, arched passages, rooftop terraces and views south over the plain change at every turn. The best light is in the morning and late afternoon. The bazaar lanes below the main road have working silversmiths, spice sellers and cloth merchants. Go on a weekday when the markets are most active. The city is best understood on foot and slowly.

02. Kasımiye Medresesi
Kasımiye Medresesi

The largest of Mardin's old Islamic schools, built in the 15th century during the Artuqid period. The courtyard has a central pool, two iwans and stone carving of exceptional quality across every surface. At the back, a terrace gives a direct view south over the Mesopotamian plain. The medrese functioned as a school until the 20th century. Entry is free. Relatively few visitors make it here, especially in the late afternoon when the light falls directly on the carved stone.

03. Zinciriye Medresesi
Zinciriye Medresesi

A 14th-century Artuqid medrese housing the tomb of Sultan Kasım, on the main road in the upper old city. The carved stone portal is one of the finest examples of Artuqid decorative stonework. Behind it, a terrace gives a panoramic view across the city and the plain. Open daily, entry is modest. Walk here in the same morning route as the Kasımiye Medresesi, continuing east along the main street.

04. Deyrulzafaran Monastery
Deyrulzafaran

One of the oldest continuously functioning monasteries in the world, built in 493 AD on the site of an Assyrian sun temple dating to around 2000 BC. The underground sun temple chamber, with flat stone slabs held without mortar, is the most extraordinary space in the complex. Tours are guided, run every 30 minutes from 09:00 to 11:30 and 14:00 to 17:30, and cost 150 TL. Take a taxi from the city — about 10 minutes. 5 km southeast on the D955 road toward Nusaybin.

05. Ulu Cami
Grand Mosque

The 12th-century Great Mosque of Mardin, built during the Artuqid period and one of the earliest examples of Syrian Islamic architecture in the region. The minaret, with its elaborately carved geometric bands, dominates the skyline when Mardin is seen from below on the plain. The prayer hall incorporates columns from earlier Byzantine and Roman structures. Open outside prayer times. Entry is free. Dress appropriately.

06. Mardin Museum
Mardin Museum

Housed in a restored late-Ottoman mansion with carved stone facades, the museum covers the archaeology and ethnography of the Mardin region from the Neolithic through the Ottoman period. The collection includes Syriac manuscripts, metalwork, ancient seals and figurines from local excavations at sites including Dara and surrounding areas. Open daily except Monday. Entry is inexpensive. Allow 90 minutes.

Local Cuisine

What to Eat

Mardin's food is the meeting point of Arabic, Kurdish and Syriac cooking traditions, shaped by centuries on the trade route between Anatolia and Mesopotamia. The spice palette is wider than the Turkish norm. Stuffed vegetables use bulgur rather than rice. Lamb and legumes dominate. Local herb production at altitude gives many dishes a sharpness you notice immediately.

Mardin Signature
Mardin Kebabı

Finely minced lamb with pistachios, cinnamon and local spices, shaped on a flat skewer and cooked over charcoal. The pistachio and cinnamon combination is specific to the Mardin kitchen and sets it apart from other southeastern kebabs. Served on flatbread with roasted tomato and grilled pepper. Order it for lunch from the restaurants in the old bazaar area.

Stuffed Dish
Mardin Dolması

Mardin dolması uses bulgur wheat rather than rice and is made with a wider range of vegetables: vine leaves, chard, pepper, tomato and dried aubergine. The filling includes ground lamb, pine nuts, currants and herbs. The tamarind-based sauce used in some preparations is a Mesopotamian influence not found elsewhere in Turkish cooking. Available at family-style restaurants in the old city.

Warm Soup
Merduma

A thick lentil and bulgur preparation made with dried red pepper and olive oil, finished with fried onions and local dried mint. The consistency is closer to a porridge than a soup and it is eaten as a main meal, particularly in winter. The combination of red lentil and coarse bulgur is an old Mesopotamian preparation. Found at lokanta-style restaurants and rooftop venues in the old city.

Meze
Cevizli Ezme

Walnut paste with red pepper, garlic, pomegranate molasses and Mardin's local dried herbs, spread on bread or eaten as a dip alongside other mezes. The walnut cultivation on the surrounding Tur Abdin hills gives the local version a freshness that distinguishes it. Served at the beginning of any proper Mardin meal. The walnut and pomegranate combination is distinctly regional.

Traditional Pastry
Kadayıf Dolması

Shredded wheat pastry rolled around crushed walnut or pistachio, fried in butter and soaked in a light syrup with orange blossom water. The Mardin version uses locally sourced pistachios and less sugar than the standard preparation. Available at pastane shops in the bazaar and at good hotels as a dessert. Best eaten fresh and warm from the pan.

Local Drink
Syriac Wine

The Syriac Christian community of the Tur Abdin has produced wine in the region since antiquity. The Deyrulzafaran Monastery sells its own wine called Dzafaran in the gift shop. Several boutique producers in Midyat and surrounding villages make red wine from indigenous grape varieties. Available at the monastery and at specialist shops in the old city. A specific souvenir of this corner of Turkey.

Experiences

Things to Do

01
Walk the Old City at Sunrise

The honey stone of Mardin turns extraordinary in early morning light. Start before 07:00, walk from the Zinciriye Medresesi west along the main road, then descend into the bazaar lanes. By 09:00 the silver workshops open and the food stalls appear. The view south from any high terrace at this hour, with the plain lit and mist still on the horizon, is not replicable at any other time of day.

02
Deyrulzafaran and the Sun Temple

Take a taxi 5 km southeast on the D955. Tours leave every 30 minutes from 09:00 and last about 40 minutes, including the underground sun temple chamber. Ask the guide specifically about the ceiling construction: flat stones held without mortar, a window designed to channel the first light of the sun. Budget 90 minutes total including waiting time. The visit is mandatory for anyone spending time in Mardin.

03
Telkari Silver in the Bazaar

Mardin is the centre of telkari, the traditional filigree silverwork made in this city for centuries. The silver bazaar below the main road has workshops where you can watch the process and buy from the maker. Silver wire is drawn to extraordinary thinness and woven into precise geometric patterns by hand. A small piece takes days to make. Buy from the workshops, not from the tourist shops on the main road above.

Beyond the City

Day Trips

30 km south
Dara Antik Kenti

The ruins of the East Roman fortress city of Daras, built in 507 AD on the Persian frontier. The underground burial chambers carved into the bedrock are the highlight — a network of tunnels and vaulted chambers still being excavated. The cisterns, city walls and colonnaded street are also visible. Free entry. Best combined with the Deyrulzafaran on the same day. The drive south through the plain is straightforward.

65 km east
Midyat and Mor Gabriel

Midyat is the other stone city of the Tur Abdin plateau, with its own old quarter, bazaar and silverwork tradition. 20 km further east, Mor Gabriel Monastery was founded in 397 AD and is the oldest functioning Syriac Orthodox monastery in the world. The guided tour covers the original 5th-century church and the tombs of the founding bishops. The drive through the Tur Abdin hills takes about an hour from Mardin.
