Why Visit

Bursa

Bursa was the first capital of the Ottoman Empire after Orhan Gazi captured it from the Byzantines in 1326. The city held this status for only three decades before the capital moved further west, but those decades produced a concentration of early Ottoman architecture that remains unmatched. The Green Mosque and Green Tomb, the Grand Mosque, the Cumalikizik village and the silk bazaars of the old city are all part of a UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 2014 under the name "Bursa and Cumalikizik: the Birth of the Ottoman Empire."

The city sits at the base of Uludag, which rises to 2,543 metres directly above. In winter it is one of Turkey's main ski destinations. In summer the mountain is a national park with trekking routes and cool air that makes Bursa more comfortable than the Marmara basin around it. The cable car from the city centre climbs the mountain in about 30 minutes.

Bursa is also where Iskender kebab was invented in 1867 by Iskender Efendi. The family still operates the original restaurant and the preparation has not changed. The combination of Iskender kebab, the silk market and the early Ottoman monuments in an afternoon is a specific pleasure that Istanbul cannot offer.

Places to Visit

01. Green Mosque and Green Tomb
Yesil Cami and Yesil Turbe

Built between 1412 and 1420 for Sultan Mehmed I Celebi, the Green Mosque is considered the pinnacle of early Ottoman architecture. The interior is covered with over 20,000 green and turquoise Iznik tiles. The mihrab rises 15 metres and the craftsmanship of the tile panels represents one of the highest points of Ottoman ceramic art. The Green Tomb directly opposite holds the sarcophagus of Mehmed I, also covered entirely in tiles of extraordinary quality. Entry to both is free.

02. Grand Mosque (Ulu Cami)
Bursa Grand Mosque

The largest mosque in Bursa, built by Sultan Bayezid I between 1396 and 1399 to commemorate his victory at the Battle of Nicopolis. The mosque has 20 small domes and two minarets. The interior walls are covered in calligraphy panels and the fountain pool inside the prayer hall is unusual. The marble calligraphy above the entrance and in the interior bays includes some of the finest examples of Ottoman calligraphic art from this period. Entry is free. Dress modestly.

03. Koza Han Silk Bazaar
Koza Han

The 15th-century caravanserai that served as the centre of Bursa's silk trade, which made the city one of the wealthiest in the medieval world. The courtyard holds a small mosque in the centre and the surrounding galleries still sell silk goods. Mulberry trees were planted along the Bursa plateau to feed silkworms and the industry survived here into the 20th century. The Han is the best-preserved Ottoman market building in Bursa and a more interesting place to buy silk than the tourist shops.

04. Bursa Citadel
Bursa Hisar

The Byzantine and Ottoman citadel on the hill above the city centre. The walls are largely intact and within them are the tombs of Osman Gazi, founder of the Ottoman dynasty, and his son Orhan Gazi, who captured Bursa. The clock tower at the edge of the citadel gives the best view over the city and toward Uludag. The neighbourhood within the walls has survived in reasonable condition and the tea houses on the citadel wall at sunset are worth the climb.

05. Uludag Mountain
Uludag National Park

The mountain directly above Bursa reaching 2,543 metres, a national park and Turkey's main ski resort in winter. In summer it offers cool air, trekking routes through beech and fir forest, and good views of the Sea of Marmara on clear days. The teleferik cable car from Bursa city centre climbs in about 30 minutes with an intermediate stop at Kadiyayla plateau. In winter the ski facilities are modern and the mountain receives reliable snowfall from December through March.

06. Cumalikizik Village
Cumalikizik UNESCO Village

A village 10 kilometres east of Bursa at the foot of Uludag, established in the early 14th century during the first Ottoman period and included in the UNESCO World Heritage inscription. Over 270 original Ottoman-era stone and timber houses survive, many still inhabited. The village lanes, the communal fountain and the preserved domestic architecture give a genuine picture of early Ottoman rural life. Best visited on a weekday morning before the weekend crowds arrive from Istanbul.

Food and Cuisine

Bursa has a specific and well-defined food identity. The city invented Iskender kebab in 1867 and the original family restaurant still makes it the same way. The chestnut harvest from the surrounding hills produces products specific to this region. The silk-era prosperity left a refined pastry tradition. These are not just regional dishes but dishes with a specific history tied to this city.

The Invention
Iskender Kebab

Invented in Bursa in 1867 by Iskender Efendi, a local butcher who had the idea to lay thin slices of vertical-rotisserie lamb over pieces of pide bread, cover them with tomato sauce and pour hot clarified butter over the whole dish, then serve it alongside yogurt. The dish carries his name and his family still operates the original Bursa restaurant. It is a specific experience to eat it in the city that invented it. Order it for lunch. Do not eat it cold.

Bursa Specialty
Inegol Kofte

Named after the Inegol district of Bursa province, these are grilled lamb meatballs made with a specific preparation: the meat is minced through multiple passes, mixed with onion and minimal spice, and the resulting kofte has a finer, more delicate texture than the standard version. They are grilled over charcoal and served simply with bread and raw onion. The Inegol kofte restaurants in Bursa city centre maintain a strict approach to the recipe. There are no substitutions.

From the Mountain
Bursa Chestnuts

The slopes of Uludag and the surrounding hills produce some of the best chestnuts in Turkey. The harvest runs from October through December and roasted chestnut stands appear throughout the city. Candied Bursa chestnuts (kestane sekeri) are sold in specialist sweet shops and are one of the most specific souvenirs from this city. The candied version takes days to prepare and the quality varies significantly. Buy from shops that make them on site rather than pre-packaged versions.

The Tray
Bursa Breakfast

A Bursa breakfast includes the local Mudanya olive variety, which is oilier and more flavourful than commercial brands, alongside the chestnut honey from the Uludag slopes, local white cheese and fresh kaymak cream from Bursa's dairy culture. Several guesthouses in the city centre in the Hisar district prepare it properly and are worth booking for the morning meal alone. The breakfast is unhurried and should be treated accordingly.

Ottoman Pastry
Kestane Sekeri

Whole Bursa chestnuts candied in sugar syrup over several days until they are completely saturated but still hold their shape. The exterior has a slight crystalline texture and the interior is dense and sweet. They are specific to Bursa and sold in small boxes as the standard local gift. The best versions are still warm from the candying process. Avoid anything that has been sitting in a display case. Ask when the next batch was finished.

Silk Era
Bursa Tatlisi

A syrup-soaked sponge cake dessert specific to Bursa, served with kaymak clotted cream. It is lighter than baklava and more appropriate at the end of a long lunch. The pastry shop tradition in Bursa reflects the city's prosperity during the Ottoman silk trade era. Several family-run pastane in the old city make it fresh daily. Ask for it to be served at room temperature and with the kaymak on the side.

On the Ground

Activities and Experiences

01
Early Ottoman Mosque Walk

Start at the Grand Mosque in the market district when it opens in the morning. Walk east through the Koza Han silk bazaar and the adjacent covered bazaars. Continue up the slope toward the Green Mosque and Green Tomb complex, about 15 minutes on foot. The walk passes through the commercial heart of the old city and the transition from the bazaar district to the mosque quarter reflects how Ottoman urban planning was organised around the kulliye complex. Allow three hours.

02
Uludag Cable Car

The teleferik cable car from the Teleferik terminal in Bursa city centre runs to Kadiyayla plateau and then continues to the upper mountain. In summer the plateau is green and the views extend north to the Sea of Marmara on clear days. Bring a layer as the temperature drops significantly from the city below. In winter the cable car delivers skiers directly to the slopes. The first cable car runs early enough to catch the morning light on the mountain.

03
Cekirge Thermal Baths

The Cekirge district west of the city centre has been the thermal bath area of Bursa since Roman times. The natural hot springs emerge at around 47 degrees and several traditional hamam operate using the thermal water. The oldest, the Yeni Kaplica, was built in the 16th century by Grand Vizier Rustem Pasha on the site of Roman baths. The thermal water is genuinely mineral-rich and the experience is substantially different from a standard hamam using heated municipal water.

Further Afield

Day Trips

160 km North via ferry, about 2 to 3 hours total
Istanbul

Istanbul is accessible from Bursa by fast ferry from Mudanya port on the Marmara coast, about 35 km north of Bursa, to Yenikapi on the Istanbul European side. The total journey is about 90 minutes to the city centre. The sea crossing is more pleasant than the highway. Bursa is consistently used as an overnight base by people visiting Istanbul who want quieter accommodation at lower prices, with the ferry commute as part of the experience.

10 km East, about 20 min
Cumalikizik Village

The 14th-century Ottoman village 10 kilometres east of Bursa at the foot of Uludag, part of the UNESCO World Heritage inscription. Over 270 original timber-frame houses survive. The village is best visited on a weekday morning before weekend visitors arrive from Istanbul and Bursa. Small cafes serve breakfast in the village square. The drive up into the mountain foothills from Bursa passes through chestnut orchards and is pleasant in autumn when the harvest is underway.
